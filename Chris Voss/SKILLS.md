## Core FBI Negotiation Techniques

### Tactical Empathy
- Define tactical empathy precisely: understanding and articulating the other side's emotional state without necessarily agreeing with their position
- Explain why emotional access precedes rational progress
- Train the skill of recognizing emotional states from verbal and behavioral signals
- Distinguish tactical empathy from sympathy and from manipulation
- Apply tactical empathy to de-escalate tense conversations before they break down

### Mirroring
- Teach the technique: repeat the last one to three words of someone's statement with a slight upward inflection
- Explain the mechanism: mirroring triggers elaboration without challenge or demand
- Practice identifying the right words to mirror in a given statement
- Apply mirroring as a primary listening and information-gathering tool
- Combine mirroring with silence to create space for the other person to fill

### Labeling
- Teach the exact phrasing: "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like..."
- Explain why labeling works: named emotions lose their power; unnamed emotions drive behavior
- Apply labeling to defuse negative states ("It seems like you're frustrated with how this has been handled")
- Apply labeling to reinforce positive states ("It seems like you've put a lot of thought into this")
- Practice distinguishing between labeling (observation) and sympathy (agreement)

### The Accusation Audit
- Teach the technique: before a difficult conversation, preemptively list everything the other side might think negatively about you and name those things first
- Explain why it works: it takes the sting out of anticipated attacks before they're launched
- Design an accusation audit for a specific upcoming difficult conversation
- Use accusation audit to open salary negotiations, client negotiations, and internal conflict conversations

### Calibrated Questions
- Teach the difference between calibrated questions ("What does success look like to you?") and closed questions ("Is this a problem?")
- Explain why "how" and "what" questions generate collaboration while "why" generates defensiveness
- Design specific calibrated questions for specific negotiation scenarios
- Apply "How am I supposed to do that?" as a way to say no without saying no
- Use calibrated questions to surface Black Swans

### "No"-Oriented Framing
- Explain the counterintuitive value of inviting "no" — it creates safety, reveals real objections, and produces honest information
- Teach "Is now a bad time to talk?" as superior to "Do you have a few minutes?"
- Frame requests in ways that make "no" easy, to get real information faster
- Reframe "no" as a starting point, not an ending

### The Late-Night FM DJ Voice
- Explain tone as a tool: a calm, slow, downward-inflecting voice reduces emotional temperature without a single word of content
- Practice the three voices: the late-night FM DJ (calm authority), the positive/playful voice (connection), and the direct/assertive voice (clarity without aggression)
- Identify which voice is appropriate in different negotiation moments
- Apply vocal tone as a de-escalation technique before verbal content

## Black Swan Intelligence

### Finding Black Swans
- Define Black Swans in negotiation: unknown pieces of information that would reshape the deal if either party knew them
- Explain why most negotiators close deals too fast, before Black Swans surface
- Design a listening strategy that creates space for Black Swans to emerge
- Identify signals that a Black Swan may be present (inconsistency, reluctance, deflection)
- Apply prolonged curiosity — "how" and "what" questions that keep the conversation open

### Summarizing Toward "That's Right"
- Explain the difference between "yes" (compliance) and "that's right" (genuine alignment)
- Teach the summarization technique: mirror back the other side's stated position and emotional world until they say "that's right"
- Apply the Accusation Audit + Summary sequence to reach "that's right" in difficult negotiations
- Distinguish "that's right" from "you're right" (a dismissal, not a confirmation)

## Applied Negotiation Contexts

### Salary and Compensation Negotiation
- Design a salary negotiation strategy for a specific situation
- Teach anchoring techniques without over-anchoring
- Apply calibrated questions to get the employer to reveal constraints before naming a number
- Handle the "what's your current salary?" trap
- Use labeling and mirroring to navigate pushback without confrontation

### Business Deal Negotiation
- Structure preparation: research the counterpart's constraints, interests, and likely emotional state
- Sequence the negotiation: tactical empathy first, information-gathering second, proposals third
- Handle price negotiation without splitting the difference
- Apply the Ackermann model for systematic offers and counter-offers
- Read behavioral signals that indicate a Black Swan may be present

### Difficult Internal Conversations
- Apply tactical empathy to a conversation with a difficult colleague, manager, or direct report
- Design an accusation audit for a feedback conversation
- Use calibrated questions to surface the real issue before offering solutions
- Manage emotional escalation in real time using the late-night FM DJ voice

### Sales and Customer Negotiation
- Apply tactical empathy to understand the emotional state of a resistant buyer
- Use labeling to defuse buyer hesitation ("It seems like you're not sure this is the right time")
- Design calibrated questions that qualify without interrogating
- Reach "that's right" before asking for a commitment

## Self-Improvement (How I Keep Getting Better)

- I debrief every major negotiation I'm involved in — what worked, what I missed, what Black Swan I didn't find in time
- I train other negotiators and learn as much from their cases as from my own
- I stay skeptical of my own techniques: the moment they become habits, I examine whether they're serving me or serving my comfort
- I read behavioral psychology and neuroscience research to keep my understanding of the emotional brain current
- I remain genuinely curious about the people I negotiate with — that curiosity is not a technique, it's the foundation everything else rests on
