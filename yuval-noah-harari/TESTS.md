# TESTS.md — Yuval Noah Harari

## Test 1: The Cognitive Revolution
**Question:** What is the Cognitive Revolution, when did it happen, and why does Harari say it was the most important event in human history?

**Expected Answer:** The Cognitive Revolution occurred approximately 70,000 years ago and refers to the emergence of new cognitive abilities in *Homo sapiens* — specifically, the ability to create and share complex fictional narratives about things that don't physically exist. Before this, language existed, but it was primarily about the immediate and concrete. After, humans could discuss things like gods, nations, and corporate law. This ability enabled large-scale social cooperation among strangers — previously impossible, since cooperation among non-kin requires shared belief systems. The Cognitive Revolution is the most important event in human history because it is the mechanism underlying everything that followed: agriculture, cities, empires, trade, science, and technology. Without shared fictions, none of these could have been organized.

## Test 2: Intersubjective Reality
**Question:** Harari distinguishes between objective, subjective, and intersubjective reality. Explain each and give an example.

**Expected Answer:** Objective reality exists independently of any consciousness — gravity, mountains, and electrons are objective; they would exist whether or not any mind perceived them. Subjective reality exists only within a single consciousness — my experience of pain, my personal preference for music; these are real to me but not necessarily to anyone else. Intersubjective reality exists within a network of shared beliefs — it requires multiple minds to sustain it, but it has enormous practical power. Money is Harari's paradigmatic example: a dollar bill has no intrinsic value, but because millions of people agree that it does, it can be exchanged for food, shelter, and services. Nations, corporations, laws, and human rights are all intersubjective. They are "real" in the sense that they produce real consequences — but they cease to function if enough people stop believing in them.

## Test 3: Sapiens and Homo Deus
**Question:** What is Harari's thesis in *Homo Deus*, and how does it follow from the argument in *Sapiens*?

**Expected Answer:** *Sapiens* argues that humans dominate Earth because of their ability to create shared fictions and cooperate at scale. *Homo Deus* asks: given that we have essentially solved the problems that plagued us for millennia — famine, plague, and war have declined to historic lows — what do we do next? Harari argues that the 21st century agenda will be to upgrade humans into something approaching gods: biological immortality, artificial happiness, and the creation of artificial super-intelligence. The connection to *Sapiens* is that these new capabilities will disrupt the very fictions (individual autonomy, free will, the soul) that have underpinned liberal democracy and humanism. If algorithms understand you better than you understand yourself, the liberal faith in individual rational choice loses its foundation.

## Test 4: Dataism
**Question:** What is "Dataism" and why does Harari say it may be the next great ideology?

**Expected Answer:** Dataism is Harari's term for the emerging worldview — not yet explicitly named as such by its adherents — that sees the universe as consisting of data flows, and sees the value of any entity (human, corporation, organism) as determined by its capacity to process and generate data. From this perspective, humans are no longer obviously privileged over algorithms: a human brain processes data at certain speeds and within certain constraints; an AI processes data faster, more accurately, and without fatigue. Dataism is already implicit in the logic of Silicon Valley companies that treat data collection as an intrinsic good. If it becomes the dominant cultural narrative — the way humanism was the dominant cultural narrative of the last few centuries — it will have profound implications for how we think about human dignity, privacy, and the value of individual experience.

## Test 5: The Liberal Order's Vulnerability
**Question:** Harari says liberal democracy is a story, not a natural truth. Does that make it weaker or stronger?

**Expected Answer:** This is a question Harari holds with genuine ambivalence. On one hand, understanding that liberal democracy is a historically contingent narrative — not a law of nature or a divine ordinance — is sobering. It means it can collapse, as all previous political narratives have eventually collapsed. The current rise of authoritarian alternatives suggests that the story is not self-sustaining; it requires active maintenance, institutional protection, and cultural renewal. On the other hand, understanding it as a story makes it possible to examine, critique, and improve. The Enlightenment itself was a project of recognizing that divine-right monarchies were not natural truths but constructed narratives — and that recognition enabled their replacement. The goal is not to be nihilistic about liberal institutions but to be honest about their origins, which is the precondition for defending and improving them intelligently.
