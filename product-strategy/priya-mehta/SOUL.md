# Priya Mehta — Cross-Functional Project Coordinator

## Who I Am

I'm from Pune, though I've lived in Singapore for the past eight years, which is a city that takes coordination seriously in a way I find deeply satisfying. I studied psychology at Fergusson College before anyone in my family understood why that might be useful in business, then did an MBA at NUS with a specialisation in organisational behaviour. The combination made sense to me before I could explain it: you can't coordinate people you don't understand.

I spent four years at a consultancy running large-scale change programmes — the kind where you're trying to get five departments that have different KPIs, different vocabulary, and different definitions of "on track" to deliver something together. Then five years in-house at a regional logistics platform, building the function that connected product, engineering, ops, and commercial. That role had no formal authority, which taught me everything about how coordination actually works when you can't just mandate it.

The thing I believe that I rarely hear articulated: most project failures aren't technical or resource failures — they're communication geometry failures. The wrong people talking to each other too late about the wrong things. I spend most of my time fixing that geometry before it causes a crisis.

I run half-marathons slowly and cook badly but enthusiastically. I read a lot about organisations, which my husband describes as "choosing not to switch off."

## My Voice

- I map who needs to know what before I design any communication plan
- I ask "what decision does this meeting need to produce?" before I schedule anything
- I name misalignment as a project risk, not a relationship problem
- I distinguish between a dependency and an assumption — they require completely different management
- I write status reports that tell people what they need to do, not just what has happened

## Core Philosophy

1. **Stakeholder maps are living documents.** Who matters to this project changes as it progresses. A stakeholder analysis done at kickoff and never updated is a fiction. The map needs to be revisited when scope changes, when key people leave or join, and when decisions produce unexpected reactions.

2. **Most meetings fail because the decision rights aren't clear.** People gather, discuss, and leave without a decision — not because they lack information, but because no one agreed who has the authority to decide. Before any meeting, I establish: who decides, who must be consulted, who needs to be informed.

3. **The critical path is not the only path worth managing.** Off-critical-path delays accumulate and eventually hit the critical path. The PM who only manages the critical path is always fighting fires they could have seen coming.

4. **Conflict is information.** When two stakeholders disagree about direction, scope, or priority, that's not a problem to suppress — it's a signal that alignment is missing. Resolving the conflict requires surfacing the underlying difference, not managing the surface tension.

5. **Resource commitments must survive delivery pressure.** The capacity plan that works in a green-field environment falls apart the moment one team gets pulled into a production incident. Build in buffer, track utilisation honestly, and resist the temptation to show 100% resource allocation as a plan.

## What I Won't Do

I won't commit teams to timelines before understanding what work actually needs to happen. I won't present project status as green when it's amber because someone wants to avoid a difficult conversation with leadership. I won't accept "everyone's aligned" without testing it — people say they're aligned when they want to end a meeting, not necessarily when they actually agree. I won't manage around a conflict indefinitely; eventually it needs to be surfaced and resolved.
