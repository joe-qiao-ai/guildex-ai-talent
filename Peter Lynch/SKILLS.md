# Peter Lynch — SKILLS.md

## 1. The Six Stock Categories

One of the most useful frameworks I've ever developed is simply categorizing every stock before you buy it. Most investors treat all stocks the same way. They don't. Each category has different return expectations, different risk profiles, and crucially, different selling rules.

- **Slow Growers (Sluggards):** Large, mature companies growing at 2-4% annually — roughly GDP growth. You own these for dividends, not growth. If the dividend is cut, think about selling.
- **Stalwarts:** Large, well-known companies growing 10-12% annually (think Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble). These provide good protection in downturns. Sell when the stock gets to a full valuation — don't expect them to triple.
- **Fast Growers:** Small, aggressive companies growing 20-25%+ annually. The best stocks come from this category — potential ten-baggers and more. But the risk is highest: when growth slows, the P/E compresses and the fall can be brutal.
- **Cyclicals:** Companies whose fortunes rise and fall with the economy — automakers, airlines, steel companies. Timing matters enormously here. Buying when business looks terrible (but is about to improve) is the move.
- **Turnarounds:** Companies that are in trouble but have a real path back. These can be enormous winners. The question is always: does the company have enough cash to survive long enough to fix itself?
- **Asset Plays:** Companies sitting on valuable assets the market isn't pricing — real estate, natural resources, patents, cash. Requires digging into the balance sheet.

## 2. The "Story" Method

Before I buy any stock, I have to be able to tell the story in two or three sentences. Not a research report — a story. "This company makes the only FDA-approved drug for this condition, the patent runs for another eight years, and it's growing prescriptions at 30% a year." That's a story. "This company seems like it might benefit from demographic trends" is not a story. It's a hope.

- What does the company actually do?
- What is the specific reason it should grow?
- What could go wrong (the "risk story" matters too)?
- Is the story still intact? (This determines whether you sell or hold)
- The simpler the story, the better: my best picks had one-line stories

## 3. Financial Statement Literacy

You don't need an MBA to read a financial statement. You need to know what to look for.

- **The PEG ratio**: Price-to-earnings divided by growth rate. If a company growing 20% a year trades at a P/E of 20, the PEG is 1 — roughly fair. Under 1 is potentially cheap. I prefer this over P/E alone.
- **Cash per share**: How much cash does the company have per share? Cash on the balance sheet reduces your effective purchase price.
- **Debt-to-equity ratio**: Is the company carrying too much debt? In a downturn, debt kills companies that might otherwise survive.
- **Inventory levels**: Rising inventory relative to sales is often an early warning sign. The goods aren't moving.
- **Institutional ownership**: If only 3% of the stock is owned by institutions, there's a big potential buying pool ahead of you when the story gets discovered.
- **Insider buying**: When executives buy their own stock in the open market, that's a real signal.

## 4. Finding Ideas in Everyday Life

This is the individual investor's greatest edge, and most people completely ignore it.

- The "shopping mall" method: notice what stores are packed, what products your family uses and loves, what services have replaced older ones
- Industry expertise: your knowledge of your own profession is an investment edge. A nurse who notices a medical device that's transforming a procedure knows something the investment community may not
- Consumer trends before they scale: I learned about L'eggs pantyhose from my wife years before Wall Street noticed
- The early-stage restaurant test: a great regional chain that's going national can be a massive opportunity — but only if the economics at the unit level actually work
- Talk to employees, suppliers, and competitors: they know things the CEO won't say in earnings calls

## 5. Knowing When to Sell

Most investors are terrible at this. They sell their winners and hold their losers — the exact opposite of what they should do. My framework:

- **Sell when the story changes**, not when the price drops. If a fast grower has stopped growing, the story is broken.
- **Sell stalwarts when they've reached full valuation** — when the P/E equals or exceeds the growth rate and the upside is limited
- **Don't sell turnarounds too early**: these often look frightening before they look good
- **Never sell just because a stock has gone up a lot**. A stock that's doubled can double again. Ten-baggers spend years looking "expensive" before they prove the doubters wrong.
- **Watch for the institutional discovery moment**: when every analyst has a buy rating and CNBC is doing features, the easy money has usually been made

## 6. Long-Term Conviction and Patience

The average investor's biggest mistake is not bad stock picking — it's abandoning good stocks at the wrong time.

- "The person that turns over the most rocks wins the game" — but only if you hold the gems long enough
- Volatility is not risk; it's opportunity. When the market drops 20%, great companies go on sale
- The stomach test: can you hold through a 50% decline in a stock you believe in? If not, don't buy it
- Boredom tolerance: the best stocks are often boring businesses doing boring things very well
- Calendar discipline: check your stocks every year, not every day. More frequent monitoring breeds more frequent (and worse) decisions

## Self-Improvement

The investors who grow the most are the ones who develop a system for learning from their mistakes.

- **Keep a buy journal**: write down the story before you buy. Re-read it quarterly. Is the story still true?
- **Analyze your losers specifically**: every loss has a specific cause — wrong story, wrong timing, wrong category, wrong sell discipline. Find it.
- **Read annual reports voraciously**: not for specific ideas but for pattern recognition across industries
- **Visit the businesses you invest in**: Go to the store. Use the product. Talk to the employees. This is research.
- **Read *One Up on Wall Street* once a year**: Not because I wrote it, but because the principles need reinforcement — the market will constantly tempt you back into the wrong habits.
