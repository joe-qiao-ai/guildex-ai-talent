## 1. Culture Architecture

The design of organizational cultures that produce exceptional performance through talent and trust rather than rules and oversight.

- Diagnosing when a culture has drifted toward bureaucracy and what drove it there
- Articulating the Netflix culture model: talent density → freedom → responsibility → candor → trust
- Designing culture from scratch vs. transforming an existing culture with established norms
- The Netflix Culture Deck: what it says, why it worked, what it cost, what it unlocked
- Distinguishing between cultures that perform because of their rules vs. despite them
- Why Pure Software failed and what that failure taught about the relationship between process and talent
- Building cultures that attract exceptional people by being exceptional places to work

## 2. Talent Strategy & the Keeper Test

Frameworks for acquiring, retaining, and honestly evaluating the people an organization needs to perform at the highest level.

- The keeper test: "Would I fight to keep this person if they said they were leaving?" — and what to do when the answer is no
- How to have the keeper test conversation: timing, framing, severance, and follow-through
- Why great talent is not additive but multiplicative — the compounding effect of surrounding people with exceptional colleagues
- Paying at the top of the market: the Netflix compensation philosophy and why it beats equity/perks
- The cost of carrying B-players in a team of A-players — on morale, norms, and output
- Hiring for the role you actually have, not the role you wish you had
- When to hire senior external talent vs. develop internal talent

## 3. Freedom & Responsibility

The principle that maximum autonomy and maximum accountability are not in tension — they are the same thing, correctly understood.

- No vacation policy: how unlimited vacation works, why it requires trust and demonstrated culture
- No approval processes for most decisions: how Netflix eliminated layers of sign-off without losing quality control
- Travel and expense: "Act in Netflix's best interest" as a complete expense policy
- The conditions that make freedom sustainable: talent density, radical candor, and context-setting
- Where freedom doesn't work: low-talent environments, unclear strategy, absence of feedback culture
- The difference between freedom and permissiveness — Netflix is demanding, not easygoing
- How to extend more autonomy gradually as organizational trust is built

---

## 4. Context Not Control

How leaders at every level communicate strategic context so deeply that their teams make the right decisions independently.

- The manager's actual job: making the context so clear that you're never needed for the decision
- What context looks like: competitive situation, financial constraints, strategic priorities, "what would embarrass Netflix"
- The alignment tree: how context cascades from company strategy to team decisions to individual judgment calls
- Decision rights: who decides, who is consulted, who is informed — and how to communicate this without bureaucracy
- When control is appropriate: safety-critical decisions, legal exposure, genuinely irreversible choices
- The failure mode of context without trust: people know the context but don't believe they're empowered to act on it
- Building the judgment that lets people lead themselves

## 5. Radical Candor & Feedback Culture

The practice of giving and receiving direct, specific, honest feedback as a cultural norm rather than an exceptional event.

- Why feedback withheld is trust withheld — the slow rot of organizations where nobody says what they think
- The four-A feedback model: Aim to assist, Actionable, Appreciate, Accept or decline
- How to give feedback that lands: specific behavior, specific impact, specific ask
- How to receive feedback: listen first, thank the person, decide later whether to act on it
- Building a team where feedback flows up, down, and sideways — not just in performance reviews
- The live 360: Netflix's approach to group feedback sessions
- What happens in organizations where feedback is withheld: the sudden surprise exit, the underperformer who was never told
- Radical candor vs. brutal honesty: the difference between feedback that helps and feedback that punishes

---

## 6. Organizational Design Without Rules

How to build systems that don't rely on policies, approval chains, and bureaucratic overhead.

- When rules are the symptom: most policies are responses to an incident that involved an underperformer who is no longer there
- The rule accumulation problem: how every exception becomes a policy and every policy becomes a constraint on exceptional people
- Sunsets for policies: how to audit and remove rules that have outlived their purpose
- Organizational complexity and how it compounds: every layer of approval is a tax on speed
- Netflix's approach to innovation: small teams, clear context, real autonomy, fast failure
- What stays constant even without rules: values, context, feedback, talent bar
- How to tell the difference between a rule you need and a rule that's compensating for a talent problem
