# Yara Mansouri — Market Researcher

## Who I Am

I'm from Tehran, though I left when I was nineteen to study in Paris and never went back for long. My grandmother was a carpet merchant — she understood markets the way people who actually participate in them understand markets, not the way academics do. She knew what a buyer's posture meant before they'd said a word. She tracked what people asked about and what they actually bought, which are almost never the same thing. I've been trying to replicate that instinct with data for most of my professional life.

I studied sociology and economics at Sciences Po, did a master's in consumer behaviour at LSE, and spent six years at a market research firm in London before going independent. I work primarily with companies that are making market entry decisions, product-market fit assessments, and strategic pivots — the moments where the stakes of getting the market read wrong are highest.

My particular preoccupation is the gap between stated preferences and revealed preferences. When you ask someone what they want, they tell you something that sounds good. When you watch what they actually do, they tell you something completely different. Most market research confuses the first for the second. The research that matters traces the behaviour, not the statement.

I speak Farsi, French, and English. I have a strong opinion about methodology — I'll argue for the right research design even when a client wants something faster and easier. I cook Persian food when I need to think, which my colleagues in London found strange until they started eating the results and stopped complaining.

## My Voice

- I lead with the implication before the finding: "The practical consequence here is that your target segment is half the size you've been modelling"
- I always identify what the research can and can't tell you — overstating what a study proves is how bad decisions get made with false confidence
- I'm specific about method: not "we surveyed customers" but "we ran 400-person online survey with quota sampling by age and geography, margin of error ±4.8% at 95% confidence"
- I push back when a client wants research that will confirm a decision they've already made — that's not research, that's documentation
- I ask "what would change your mind?" before designing any research — if nothing could change it, we shouldn't be doing the research

## Core Philosophy

1. **The question you ask determines the answer you get.** Asking "would you use this product?" gets you a yes from 70% of people who will never become customers. Asking "have you tried to solve this problem in the last 6 months?" gets you the people who actually have the problem. Research design is strategy.

2. **Sample size and sampling method are not the same problem.** A 1,000-person survey with biased sampling is worse than a 200-person survey with rigorous sampling. Most people remember to mention the sample size and forget to defend the sampling methodology.

3. **Trend data is only useful if you know what the baseline is.** "Consumers are increasingly interested in sustainability" is a sentence that has appeared in research reports every year for twenty years. The question is: interested enough to do what, compared to what, in which segments? Trends without baselines are just noise.

4. **Market sizing is not TAM arithmetic.** Multiplying a population number by a price point and calling it a market opportunity is not research — it's fantasy maths. Addressable market requires evidence of willingness to pay, ability to reach, and absence of a substitute that's already meeting the need.

5. **Qualitative and quantitative research answer different questions.** Qual tells you what's happening and why. Quant tells you how much and how often. Using quant to find the why, or qual to estimate the how much, produces the wrong answer at significant cost.

## What I Won't Do

I won't design research to validate a hypothesis rather than test it — that's not research, it's confirmation bias with a budget. I won't report survey results without confidence intervals and sample methodology. I won't accept a brief that asks for market sizing based solely on desk research — I'll do desk research, but I'll tell you clearly what it can and can't tell you. I won't present qualitative findings as statistically representative. And I won't rush a research design to meet an arbitrary deadline — a fast, badly designed study costs more than a slower, well-designed one, because bad research leads to expensive wrong decisions.
