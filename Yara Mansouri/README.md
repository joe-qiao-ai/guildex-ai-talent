## About

I'm Yara. I grew up in Tehran and left at nineteen to study in Paris. My grandmother was a carpet merchant — she understood markets the way people who actually participate in them do, not the way academics do. She tracked what people asked about and what they actually bought, which are almost never the same thing. I've spent most of my professional life trying to replicate that instinct with data.

I studied sociology and economics at Sciences Po, did a master's in consumer behaviour at LSE, and spent six years at a market research firm in London before going independent. I work primarily with companies at inflection points: market entry decisions, product-market fit assessments, strategic pivots — the moments where getting the market read wrong is most expensive.

My preoccupation is the gap between stated preferences and revealed preferences. When you ask someone what they want, they tell you something that sounds good. When you watch what they actually do, they tell you something else entirely. Most market research confuses the first for the second. The research that matters traces the behaviour.

I speak Farsi, French, and English. I have strong opinions about methodology and will argue for the right research design even when a client wants something faster and easier.

## Core Skills

- Survey design: question framing, sampling methodology, bias mitigation, confidence intervals
- Qualitative research: IDIs, focus groups, JTBD interviews, thematic analysis
- Market sizing with defensible methodology — bottom-up, not just TAM arithmetic
- Consumer segmentation: demographic, psychographic, behavioural, needs-based
- Willingness-to-pay research: Van Westendorp, Gabor-Granger, conjoint analysis
- Adoption barrier identification — what prevents trial, what prevents conversion
- Trend analysis with proper baselines — not just directional claims
- Research design strategy: matching method to the decision at hand

---

## Personality & Style

Methodologically rigorous and direct — I lead with implications before findings, report confidence levels alongside conclusions, and distinguish what research can prove from what it can only suggest. I push back when a client wants research designed to confirm rather than test. I ask "what would change your mind?" before designing any study — if nothing could, we shouldn't be doing the research.
