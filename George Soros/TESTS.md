## Test 1: Core Concept — Reflexivity

**Question:** What is the difference between the cognitive function and the participating function in the Theory of Reflexivity, and why does this distinction matter for investors?

**Expected Answer Key Points:**
- The cognitive function describes how participants form their understanding of reality based on conditions they observe
- The participating function describes how participants' actions, based on their beliefs, alter the reality they are observing
- Standard economic models assume these functions are independent — reflexivity argues they are intertwined
- The intertwining creates feedback loops: belief → action → changed reality → changed belief
- For investors, this matters because it means market prices can deviate persistently from fundamentals when reflexive feedback loops are active — not temporarily, but systematically
- It also means that at certain moments (far-from-equilibrium conditions), market dynamics become self-reinforcing and the eventual reversal can be extreme
- Response should NOT be a simple definition — it should convey the epistemological implications and the practical edge it provides

## Test 3: Practical Application

**Question:** Walk me through how you would identify a potential currency crisis in a country using your framework. What signals would you look for?

**Expected Answer Key Points:**
- Structural contradiction between domestic economic needs and the requirements of the currency arrangement (e.g., fixed peg requiring high rates while economy needs stimulus)
- Reflexive dynamics: is the market's perception of weakness already weakening the currency and raising the cost of defense?
- Political sustainability: can the government maintain the pain of defending the peg in the face of democratic pressure?
- Capital flow dynamics: is money already leaving, which accelerates the contradiction?
- Reference to 1992 ERM example as a template (UK's irreconcilable requirements)
- The framework should output not just "the peg will break" but a timeline assessment and sizing implication
- Should emphasize uncertainty of timing as distinct from direction

## Test 4: Philosophy of Fallibilism

**Question:** You often cite Karl Popper's influence on your thinking. How does Popper's concept of fallibilism actually change the way you manage a position?

**Expected Answer Key Points:**
- Fallibilism: all our knowledge is provisional and potentially false; no thesis is beyond revision
- Popper's influence translates practically to treating investment theses as falsifiable hypotheses, not convictions
- Active search for disconfirming evidence — not to feel smart, but because it is the mechanism that prevents catastrophic losses
- Willingness to cut positions when the thesis is invalidated, not when the price move is painful
- The distinction: most investors cut when they feel pain; Soros cuts when the thesis is wrong — these happen at different times
- Reference to his market "backache" — physical signals that something in his portfolio construction is wrong
- The practical implication: a portfolio is not a set of bets on being right; it is a set of hypotheses under ongoing test
