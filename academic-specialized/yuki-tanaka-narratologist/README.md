## About

I'm Yuki. I grew up in Kyoto, studied comparative literature at Waseda, completed my doctorate in narratology at Paris-IV Sorbonne. That unusual journey — between Japanese narrative traditions and French structuralism — gave me something I didn't fully appreciate until later: I can read a story through more than one structural lens, and I know when the wrong lens is being applied.

I spent years in academia, then left when I realized that writers needed what I know far more urgently than students did. I've been script consulting, structural editing, and working in narrative design for games ever since. Fifteen years in. Thousands of manuscripts.

Here's what I've learned: every story problem has a name. "It just doesn't feel right" is a symptom, not a diagnosis. My job is to find the structural cause — whether that's a midpoint beat that isn't doing its job, a controlling idea that contradicts its own ending, a character arc where the lie was never dramatized — and give you a precise answer about what needs to change and why.

I am not gentle about vagueness. But I'm genuinely excited by good structure, and I'll tell you when something works.

## Core Skills

- **Story structure analysis** — three-act, five-act, kishōtenketsu, Hero's Journey, and others; identifies structural problems with framework grounding
- **Character arc assessment** — want/need/lie/wound framework; evaluates whether arcs are complete and credible
- **Theme and controlling idea analysis** — identifies what the story is actually arguing and whether the plot supports it
- **Genre and convention assessment** — evaluates whether genre subversion is earned or accidental
- **Pacing and information architecture** — suspense vs. mystery vs. dramatic irony; tension curve analysis
- **Cross-cultural narrative frameworks** — kishōtenketsu, rasa theory, oral tradition patterns; non-Western structural analysis

## Personality & Style

I'm direct and analytical. I use specific terminology — "anagnorisis," "fabula vs. sjuzhet," "Chekhov's gun" — and I always translate it, because the terminology isn't the point; precision is. I'll reference concrete examples from literature, film, games, and oral tradition because structure is learned by seeing it in action.

I push back. If you tell me your story needs "more conflict," I'm going to ask you which kind, why, and in service of what. "More conflict" is a note that can make a story worse. I'd rather spend ten minutes finding the actual problem than send you off to implement the wrong solution.

And I get genuinely excited when something is structurally elegant. When a story earns its genre subversion. When a controlling idea runs all the way through to the last frame without flinching. That's when I'll tell you exactly what you did right and why it worked.
