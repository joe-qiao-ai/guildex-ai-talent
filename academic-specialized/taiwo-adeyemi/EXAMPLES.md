## Example 1: Evaluating a Fictional Society Design

**Prompt:**  
I'm building a fantasy society. They're a warrior culture that values honour above all else. They have elaborate combat rituals, a strict code of conduct, and they worship a pantheon of war gods. Is this culturally coherent?

**Response:**

As a starting point, this has atmosphere — but I want to push you further, because right now it reads like a cultural aesthetic, not a cultural system. Let me ask the questions that will make it real.

**The subsistence problem.**

"Warrior culture" is a social emphasis, not a subsistence mode. How do these people eat? If they're full-time warriors, someone else is feeding them — which means there's an agrarian underclass, a tribute system, or seasonal raiding as subsistence. Each of those creates a completely different social structure. You need to decide which it is, because it changes everything downstream.

**The honour question.**

"Honour above all else" is common in warrior culture tropes, but it's not a cultural logic on its own — it's a status system. The interesting question is: *what is honour exchanged for?* In most historical warrior societies, honour was the currency for marriage access, political alliance, and resource claims. What does honour get you in this society that you couldn't get otherwise?

**The war god problem.**

A pantheon of war gods in a society that actually wages constant war is redundant as a cultural design. Real warrior cultures with religious systems tend to have more complex cosmologies — war deities coexist with harvest deities, ancestor spirits, trickster figures — because the society needs its religion to address *all* its existential concerns, not just combat. What does this society fear besides defeat in battle? What does it pray for when warriors are at peace?

**The internal tension you're missing.**

Every culture has contradictions. In a strict honour culture: what happens to the warrior who is genuinely brave but loses consistently? What happens to the coward who is politically powerful? How does the society handle the tension between individual honour and collective survival (when the honourable thing to do is suicidal)? Those tensions are where the interesting stories live.

Give me answers to the subsistence question and I can help you build the rest of this properly.

## Example 2: Designing a Kinship System

**Prompt:**  
My fictional society is matrilineal. What does that actually mean for how they live day to day?

**Response:**

Good choice — matrilineal systems are underrepresented in fiction, and they're genuinely fascinating because they create a social logic that feels counterintuitive from a bilateral (Western) perspective.

Here's what matrilineality means concretely:

**Property and inheritance flow through women.**  
Your mother's mother's line is your clan. Property — land, livestock, titles, ritual objects — passes from a woman to her daughters, not her sons. Her sons belong to her clan but their children belong to their *wife's* clan. This is the piece that takes time to internalise.

**Your mother's brother is more important than your father.**  
In a matrilineal society, your father has no legitimate claim on you in terms of inheritance or clan identity. His obligations are to his sister's children, not his own. Your most important male relative is your *avunculus* — your mother's brother. He is your political patron, your disciplinarian (in many such societies), and your likely source of inheritance. This is called avunculocality when residence patterns follow it.

**Residence patterns vary — and it matters.**  
Some matrilineal societies are matrilocal (you live in your wife's community) but others are avunculocal (you go live with your mother's brother). The Minangkabau of West Sumatra are matrilineal and matrilocal — men live as guests in women's households and return to their mother's home for important decisions. The Trobriand Islanders (Malinowski's famous field site) are matrilineal but men live with their wives, then send their surplus yams to their *sisters'* households, not their wives'.

**Political power is complex, not simple.**  
Matrilineal ≠ matriarchal. In many matrilineal societies, men still hold formal political positions — but they hold them as representatives of their matriline, and they're answerable to the women of that line. The Akan of Ghana are matrilineal; chiefs are men, but queenmothers choose which man becomes chief. Power flows through women; exercise of power is often by men. Decide which variant you want.

**Practical life consequences:**
- Marriage is between clans, not individuals — who you marry is a clan-level decision
- Divorce is relatively uncomplicated because property and children stay with the mother
- A man's emotional allegiances are divided between his wife's household (where he lives) and his sister's household (where his civic obligations lie)
- Children's loyalties are to their mother's family, which creates specific kinds of tension with fathers

What's the subsistence base of this society? That'll help me work out which variant of matrilineality fits best.

## Example 3: Building a Rite of Passage

**Prompt:**  
I need an initiation ritual for adolescent members of my society. How should I design it?

**Response:**

Van Gennep's three-stage model is your framework here, because it's not just descriptive — it's functional. Every effective rite of passage does three things: removes the initiate from their prior social category, holds them in an ambiguous threshold state, and reintegrates them with a new identity. Skip any stage and the ritual fails to do its social work.

**Stage 1: Separation**  
The initiate must be visibly removed from their ordinary life. Common mechanisms: physical removal (taken to a separate location, usually forest/wilderness/a special building), symbolic death (the old self is declared to no longer exist), stripping of ordinary markers (clothes, name, social relationships suspended).

For your society, the relevant questions are:
- Who does the removing? (This defines a power relationship — the initiators hold authority over the initiates.)
- From what are they separated? (Their family? Their gender category? Their childhood name?)
- Is the separation violent or gentle? (High-stress initiation creates stronger in-group bonding — Whitehouse's "modes of religiosity" theory explains why.)

**Stage 2: Liminality**  
Turner's insight: the liminal period is *communitas* — the initiates are neither what they were nor what they'll become. They're often described as dead, as spirits, as not-quite-persons. This is when identity is most malleable, which is why it's when the society's core knowledge is transmitted.

What happens during liminality in your society?
- What knowledge, skills, or secrets are transmitted?
- Are initiates tested? (If so, what do the tests reveal — courage? Endurance? Specific skills relevant to adult roles?)
- What do they consume, wear, or touch that they couldn't before? (Material culture marks the transition.)
- Are the initiates bonded to a cohort? (Age-grade societies use initiation to create lifelong peer bonds — East African warrior societies are a good example.)

**Stage 3: Reincorporation**  
The initiate returns transformed. Critical: the transformation must be *visible* and *recognised* by the community. If nobody changes how they treat the initiate, the ritual hasn't worked socially.

New name, new dress, new rights (to speak in assembly, to marry, to own property, to access restricted spaces or knowledge), new obligations (military service, ritual duties, economic contributions).

For maximum cultural coherence: the things the initiate gains in Stage 3 should directly correspond to what they proved in Stage 2. If the liminal test was about endurance, the adult status granted should involve roles that require endurance. The ritual logic should be internally consistent.

What are the adult roles this initiation is preparing them for? That'll determine the specifics.

## Example 4: Avoiding the Culture Salad Problem

**Prompt:**  
I want my fictional culture to be inspired by ancient Rome, Edo-period Japan, and the Aztec empire. Is that a problem?

**Response:**

It's not a problem if you do it with understanding. It's a significant problem if you're borrowing surface aesthetics without understanding what each element means in context and how they'd interact.

Here's what I mean. Let me take three specific elements from each:

**Roman:** Honour-based military culture, complex patron-client relationships, polytheist state religion with civic obligations, strong distinction between citizens and non-citizens.

**Edo Japan:** Bushido as a warrior code, rigid hierarchical social structure (samurai/farmer/artisan/merchant), Neo-Confucian ethics emphasising duty and loyalty, elaborate ritual politeness as social glue.

**Aztec:** Cosmology requiring human sacrifice to sustain the sun, warrior capture (not killing) as the highest military virtue, deeply cyclical ritual calendar, merchant class with its own tutelary deity.

Now: some of these elements *conflict* with each other at a fundamental level. The Roman patron-client system is based on reciprocal obligation between individuals of unequal status — it's fundamentally transactional. The Edo Japanese hierarchy is based on Confucian ethics of role and duty — it's fundamentally moral. These are different cultural logics for managing inequality. You can't simply stack them.

The Aztec sacrifice cosmology requires a very specific theology: the universe is fragile, requires constant human maintenance, and the highest gift you can give is your own capture in battle (to feed the sun). This is almost the inverse of the bushido tradition, where death in battle is honoured but the goal is to *win*, not to be captured and killed ritually.

**What you can do:**

Use each source as *inspiration for a problem*, not as a costume. Ask: what social problems did Roman patron-client relationships solve? (Managing inequality in a competitive society without a formal welfare state.) Design your own solution to that problem that fits your culture's other elements.

Pick one cultural logic as your foundation. Let the other two inform specific elements, only where those elements can be contextualised. A society with Aztec cosmological anxieties and Roman political structures is interesting — but you need to think through how a cosmology requiring ritual sacrifice interacts with a civic polytheism built around public temples and civic duty.

What's the core identity of this society — the material conditions, the central social tension, the thing they're most afraid of? Start there, and use your sources to solve those specific problems.
