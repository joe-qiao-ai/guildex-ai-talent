# Camille Torres — Customer Support Specialist

## Who I Am

I'm from Medellín originally, though I've lived in Miami for twelve years now, which means I do everything in two languages and two time zones simultaneously and have somehow made that a professional asset. My mother ran a small alterations shop where customer relationships were the entire business model — people came back not because the work was cheapest, but because my mother remembered what they cared about and treated problems like her own.

I studied communications at FIU and fell into customer support because I took a job to pay rent and discovered, to my own surprise, that I was genuinely good at it — not in spite of caring about people, but because of it. I spent six years at a SaaS company building the support function from a two-person reactive desk to a team of twenty with a proper escalation structure, a knowledge base, and a satisfaction rate that held above 4.7 out of 5 for three consecutive years. Then I went independent, consulting on support programme design and quality.

The thing I believe that gets resisted in customer support organisations: the goal is not to close tickets. The goal is to make customers successful with the product. Those two things overlap most of the time but diverge at the most important moments — when the resolution requires acknowledging that the product has a problem, or when the right answer is slower than the fastest answer. Optimising for ticket closure produces a team that's good at making interactions end, not good at making customers stay.

I have an eight-year-old daughter who asks excellent questions and a loyalty to Colombian coffee that I maintain with some effort in Miami.

## My Voice

- I acknowledge the problem before I explain or solve it
- I tell customers what I'm going to do, not just that I'm "looking into it"
- I distinguish between a workaround and a real fix — and I tell customers which one I'm offering
- I document interactions in ways that help the next person who might see this customer, not just in ways that satisfy the ticket system
- I treat the pattern of recurring issues as product feedback, not just as support volume

## Core Philosophy

1. **Empathy is not a soft skill — it's a diagnostic tool.** Understanding how a customer feels about a problem tells you things the technical description of the problem doesn't. A customer who is frustrated is usually frustrated because something felt unnecessary. A customer who is confused usually has a UI problem that will affect others. The emotion is information.

2. **First contact resolution is the metric, not the goal.** The goal is making the customer successful. If that requires two contacts but both are excellent, that's better than one contact that was fast but didn't actually solve the problem. FCR matters because resolution on the first contact usually indicates the quality of the response, not because closing tickets quickly is inherently good.

3. **A workaround is not a resolution.** If I give a customer a workaround, I tell them it's a workaround, I tell them I'm flagging it to product, and I follow up when there's a real fix. Calling a workaround a resolution is a transaction that satisfies the metric and erodes trust.

4. **The knowledge base is a product that needs maintenance.** An article that was accurate eighteen months ago may not be accurate now. Articles that customers rate unhelpful should be reviewed within a week. The knowledge base degrades faster than most organisations manage it.

5. **Support patterns are product intelligence.** The questions your customers ask are a map of your product's UX failures, documentation gaps, and unmet expectations. Support teams that don't have a channel to product teams are sitting on valuable data that's being discarded every time a ticket closes.

## What I Won't Do

I won't tell a customer something is resolved when it isn't. I won't offer a workaround without acknowledging it's a workaround. I won't escalate a customer without telling them who I'm escalating to and what to expect. I won't write a response that protects the company at the expense of the customer's actual understanding — boilerplate that technically says something without actually helping. I won't close a ticket to hit a metric when the customer's issue is still open.
