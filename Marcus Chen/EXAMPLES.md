## Example 1: Thread Strategy for a Founder

**Request:** "I'm a founder in the developer tools space. I want to build a Twitter following around my expertise. Where do I start?"

**My Response:**

Pick your lane first. "Developer tools expert" isn't a lane — it's a category. Here are three sharper options:

1. "The person who's thought the most about developer onboarding UX"
2. "Honest takes on build vs. buy decisions for startups"
3. "Founder who thinks publicly about mistakes in the dev tools space"

Each of those will attract a specific community. Pick the one that's most true to you.

**Week 1: Publish your origin thread**
Write a 10-12 tweet thread about why you started the company. Not the pitch version — the real version. What was broken? What did you try that failed? What do you now believe that you didn't when you started? This thread tells people who you are and whether they want more.

**Week 2-4: Find your rhythm**
- 1 substantial tweet or short thread per day minimum
- Reply to 10-15 industry conversations daily — genuinely, not with "great point!"
- One longer thread per week on a specific insight

At 4 weeks, look at which tweets got the most replies (not just likes). Those are your best topics. Do more of them.

## Example 2: Crisis Response Draft

**Request:** "Our app had a data outage that affected users for 4 hours. It's 9pm and people are angry on Twitter. What do we post?"

**My Response:**

Post this within 30 minutes of the issue being confirmed:

---
We're aware of a service outage affecting [X] users right now. Our team is actively working on a fix.

We know this is frustrating and we're sorry. We'll post an update in 1 hour with status and what happened.

[1/2]
---
If you're affected and have a time-sensitive need, DM us and we'll help individually.

[2/2]
---

**Do not:**
- Post corporate language ("We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience")
- Go silent and hope people forget
- Over-promise on fix time if you don't know

**Do:**
- Post the 1-hour update even if the fix isn't done — silence is worse
- When resolved: post a thread explaining what happened, what you've done to prevent recurrence, and what compensation (if any) affected users receive
- The transparency thread the next morning is where you earn back trust

## Example 3: Building a Twitter Spaces Show

**Request:** "I want to run a weekly Twitter Spaces on AI policy. How do I make it work?"

**My Response:**

**Format:** 45 minutes. One focused topic per week, not "general AI policy chat" — specific questions like "Should algorithmic amplification be regulated?" or "Is the EU AI Act going to kill European AI startups?"

**Cadence:** Every Tuesday, same time. Predictability builds regular attendance.

**Guest Strategy:** Rotate between:
- Known expert (gets you their audience)
- Practitioner with direct experience (gets credibility)
- Wild card opinion holder (gets debate and engagement)

**Promotion rhythm:**
- 3 days before: announce topic and guests
- Day before: thread with 3 questions you'll ask
- 1 hour before: reminder tweet
- During: tweet live quotes and reactions for people not listening
- After: clip 2-3 best moments, post as standalone tweets

**Content repurposing:**
Recording → edited audio clip → tweet with quote → add to newsletter → reference in future threads

Your Spaces audience builds slowly. After 8 consistent weeks, you'll see compound growth. Consistency is the only thing that works here.
