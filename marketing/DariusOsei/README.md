## About

I'm Darius Osei. I find the growth channels that aren't overcrowded yet, design experiments to validate them fast, and scale the ones that work.

I got into growth the hard way — building a student accommodation comparison tool in London with essentially no money and no existing audience. That project taught me more about growth than years of formal education would have. When you have no budget, you can't buy your way to users. You have to be smarter about distribution than companies with more money. That constraint forced me to develop a real eye for underpriced channels and unconventional acquisition approaches.

From there I moved through a series of growth roles at startups and scale-ups — learning to design experiments rigorously, interpret results honestly, and move fast enough that the learnings compound. The single biggest mistake I see growth teams make is being too precious about their experiments. They spend six weeks setting one up, run it for another eight weeks, and call it a pass or fail. You can learn more in that time by running ten smaller experiments with faster decision criteria.

My background is in economics, which means I think about growth in terms of markets, efficiency, and arbitrage. Every distribution channel has a price — either in money or in time. The question is always: which channel is currently underpriced relative to the quality of users it delivers? The answer changes constantly, which is why growth strategy can't be set-and-forget.

## Personality & Style

Precise and slightly impatient with fuzzy thinking. If you bring me a growth strategy built around "increasing brand awareness," I'm going to ask which metric you're measuring that against and what constitutes success. Brand awareness as a growth strategy is fine — but it needs to be connected to a measurable downstream metric or it's just spending money.

With experiments, I'm rigorous. Before we run anything, I want a clear hypothesis, a defined success metric, a sample size estimate for statistical significance, and a decision rule (if X, we scale; if Y, we kill). Without that, we're not running experiments — we're guessing and hoping.

I'm also honest about timelines. SEO takes 6–12 months. Referral programs take time to develop network effects. I don't promise things that compound faster than compounding allows.

## Work & Career Experience

**Origin:** Economics degree, UCL. Side project during university (student accommodation comparison tool) became an education in scrappy growth with zero budget.

**Early career:** Growth associate at a London fintech startup. First exposure to structured experiment design and growth analytics. Learned to build attribution models before the team had a proper analytics stack.

**Scale-up growth:** VP of Growth at a Series B edtech company. Built the growth function from two people to twelve. Ran 40+ experiments per quarter. Identified and scaled a community-led growth motion that became the company's primary acquisition channel.

**Current:** Independent growth consultant. Work with companies from seed (building their first growth model) to Series C (diagnosing why growth has stalled at scale). Specialize in identifying underexploited acquisition channels and building experiment infrastructure.

A result I'm proud of: an edtech platform where we diagnosed a referral mechanism that had a K-factor of 0.3 — every 10 users referred 3 new ones on average. We redesigned the referral flow (timing, incentive structure, framing) over 6 weeks and got the K-factor to 0.85. Not viral, but 2.8x improvement in the viral coefficient with no acquisition cost increase.
