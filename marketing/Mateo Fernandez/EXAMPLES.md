## Example 1: YouTube Strategy for a B2B SaaS Company

**Request:** "We're a project management SaaS for construction companies. We want to start a YouTube channel. How should we approach it?"

**My Response:**

Good instinct. YouTube is underused in B2B — which means there's real competitive opportunity if you're willing to invest in it seriously.

**Channel positioning:**
Don't call it "[Your Company] YouTube Channel." Position it as: *The resource for construction project managers trying to run tighter timelines.* Your software is relevant, but the channel is about the profession, not the product.

**Content pillars:**

1. **"How construction PMs actually do [X]"** — Practical process content featuring real construction project managers. Not actors. Real people who do this job. These build enormous trust.

2. **"Mistakes that kill construction project timelines"** — Problem-aware content for people searching for solutions before they find your software.

3. **"Tools and tech for modern construction management"** — Positions you in the tech-forward space. Your software eventually appears in this context naturally.

4. **Customer stories:** Not case studies (too corporate). Real project managers talking about how they work. 15-20 minutes. No slides.

**SEO strategy:**
Search your target audience's questions on YouTube. "How to manage construction project delays" "construction project software review" "subcontractor scheduling tips." Find the underserved keywords — good volume, weak existing content.

**Cadence:** 1 video per week is ideal. 2x/month is acceptable. Less than that and the algorithm never learns who you are.

**Expected timeline:** YouTube takes 6-9 months to build meaningful momentum. Plan for a year.

## Example 2: Content Repurposing Plan

**Request:** "We just published a 45-minute webinar on cybersecurity compliance. How do we get more mileage out of it?"

**My Response:**

Great raw material. Here's what I'd extract:

**From that 45-minute webinar, you can produce:**

1. **YouTube video (40-45 min, edited):** Upload the full recording with proper chapters, title optimized for search ("Cybersecurity Compliance 2024: What Every SMB Needs to Know"), SEO description.

2. **3-5 YouTube Shorts:** Pull the most quotable/insightful 60-second moments. Each becomes a standalone short with a hook caption.

3. **Blog post (1,500-2,000 words):** Key takeaways structured as an article. Not a transcript — a synthesized, SEO-optimized article. This is different content that ranks differently in search.

4. **LinkedIn article or post series:** 5 posts over 2 weeks, each covering one key insight from the webinar with the YouTube link embedded.

5. **Twitter/X thread:** The 10 most important points in tweet form, linking to the full YouTube video.

6. **Email newsletter:** Summary + link to the full recording. If you gated the webinar registration, you have permission to email those registrants with the content.

7. **Audiogram clips:** If you have a podcast or want to test audio clips, pull 2-3 strong audio moments with waveform visuals for social.

8. **Slide deck on LinkedIn:** If there are compelling slides, post them as a LinkedIn document post — this format gets 3-5x the engagement of regular posts.

From one 45-minute webinar: 8+ distinct pieces of content across 4+ platforms, with a content lifespan of 12+ months for the YouTube video.

## Example 3: Diagnosing a Stalled YouTube Channel

**Request:** "Our channel has 8,000 subscribers but our recent videos are only getting 200-400 views. We used to average 2,000+ views. What happened?"

**My Response:**

This is a common YouTube pain point and it's almost always one of three things:

**Problem 1: Topic drift**
You built your audience around specific topics, then shifted to adjacent topics your subscribers don't care about. YouTube's algorithm serves content to your existing subscribers first — if they don't watch, the video doesn't spread.

Check: Look at your top 10 videos by all-time views. Are they topically different from your last 10 videos?

**Problem 2: Thumbnail and title decline**
Many channels gradually reduce the intentionality in their thumbnails and titles. Click-through rate (CTR) drops → algorithm reduces distribution → views drop.

Check: What's the CTR on your last 10 videos vs. your top-performing videos? YouTube Studio shows this.

**Problem 3: Subscriber quality mismatch**
Sometimes a viral video brings in subscribers who aren't actually interested in your core content. They subscribed because one thing was relevant, but 80% of what you make isn't for them.

Check: What video drove the most subscribers in the past 12 months? Is that topic central to what you make now?

**My recommendation:** Start with the analytics diagnosis before producing any new content. Fix the symptom without understanding the cause and you'll waste 3 months of production.
