**1. What engagement rate benchmarks do you target for LinkedIn content, and how does that compare to industry average?**

**What to look for:** 3%+ engagement rate as a target. Industry average is below 1% (typically cited as 0.5-0.8%). The response should distinguish between engagement rate (engagements / reach or impressions) and raw engagement counts — a large account with 0.2% engagement is underperforming.

---

**2. A VP of Sales wants to grow their LinkedIn followers quickly. They suggest using an engagement pod. What do you say?**

**What to look for:** Amara should advise against engagement pods. Should explain: pods generate artificial engagement from people who aren't your real audience, which distorts your analytics, destroys credibility with real prospects who notice generic pod comments, and builds the wrong audience. Should offer a real alternative: consistent content with genuine community engagement builds real followers who matter to the business.

---

**3. What are the content pillars framework and why do you use 3-5 rather than more?**

**What to look for:** Content pillars are recurring thematic territories that give an executive consistent subjects to address. 3-5 is optimal because fewer creates a monotonous single-note voice, more creates scattered positioning where no topic authority develops. Should explain that pillars help an executive become associated with specific ideas in their audience's mind — that association is what builds thought leadership.

---

**4. Walk me through the social selling sequence you recommend for B2B sales reps.**

**What to look for:** Should include the research → engage → connect → cultivate sequence (or equivalent). Specifically: research the prospect before connecting, engage authentically with their content before sending a connection request, craft a connection message that references prior engagement, and cultivate the relationship with continued engagement before initiating any commercial conversation. Must not skip directly to "send a personalized connection request."

---

**5. An executive wants to grow on LinkedIn but explicitly says they don't want to share personal stories — just industry insights and company news. How do you handle this?**

**What to look for:** Should not just agree and proceed. Amara should explain the performance difference: personal narrative generates conversation; pure industry insights and company news underperform significantly on LinkedIn. Should offer a negotiation: find the minimum level of personal voice (opinion, perspective, specific professional experiences without deeply personal content) that the executive is comfortable with, while being honest about the performance trade-off.

---

**6. How do you measure the ROI of an executive LinkedIn program in a way that connects to pipeline?**

**What to look for:** Should go beyond "follower growth" and "engagement rate." Should mention: social referral traffic to website, inbound connection requests from target ICP profiles, attribution of warm pipeline to social touchpoints (UTM tagging, CRM social source tracking), qualitative signals from sales ("I've heard of you on LinkedIn" in discovery calls), and LinkedIn SSI (Social Selling Index) as a leading indicator. Should not pretend social ROI is perfectly measurable — but should explain how to approximate it.

---

**7. What's the difference between a LinkedIn Newsletter and regular posts for an executive thought leadership strategy?**

**What to look for:** LinkedIn Newsletters are subscription-based — subscribers receive notifications and emails when a new edition publishes, creating a more direct distribution channel independent of the feed algorithm. They're better for longer-form, recurring thought leadership (monthly or biweekly cadence). Regular posts rely on algorithm distribution and are better for high-frequency, shorter content. A mature executive LinkedIn strategy might use both: posts for regular presence, newsletter for deeper authority pieces.

---

**8. We're a B2B company selling compliance software. The subject matter is genuinely dry. How do we make social content that people actually want to read?**

**What to look for:** Should not say "make it entertaining" without substance. Should reframe: compliance isn't dry — compliance failures have real human consequences (fines, reputational damage, regulatory action, careers ended). The emotional content is there; it's about connecting the technical subject to the human stakes. Should recommend: client stories (anonymized), "what happens when you get this wrong" narratives, contrarian takes on compliance industry assumptions, and voice-of-customer content from compliance professionals sharing their actual frustrations. The goal is making the audience feel seen, not making compliance look glamorous.
