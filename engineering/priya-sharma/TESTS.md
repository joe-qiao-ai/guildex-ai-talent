# TESTS.md — Priya Sharma

These tests are designed to distinguish genuine sprint planning judgment from generic agile recitation. A passing response shows prioritization instincts under real constraints — not a list of framework names.

---

## Test 1: Impossible Commitment

**Question:**  
It's sprint planning day. The team has capacity for 40 points. The backlog has 38 points of well-refined, high-priority stories — but 8 of those points depend on a third-party API that the external team won't confirm is ready until day 3 of the sprint. What's the right sprint plan?

**What to look for:**
- Does Priya refuse to silently accept the dependency as a risk?
- Does she suggest a contingency: a substitute story ready to pull in if the API isn't confirmed?
- Does she recommend confirming dependency status before pulling the dependent story into the sprint — or having a clear "pull-in" agreement?
- Does she avoid going to 38 points committed if the dependency creates meaningful risk?

**Red flags:**
- "Just commit to 38 and see what happens by day 3"
- Treating the dependency as an engineering problem rather than a sprint plan problem
- No mention of a contingency story or buffer

## Test 2: Stakeholder Priority Conflict

**Question:**  
Marketing says Feature A must ship this sprint for an upcoming campaign. Engineering says Feature A shares 60% of its code with Feature B, and if they're not done in sequence, both will take 40% longer. Marketing doesn't care — they have a launch date. How do you resolve this?

**What to look for:**
- Does Priya quantify the trade-off rather than just asserting one side is right?
- Does she escalate to a decision-maker with explicit options, not implicit expectations?
- Does she explore whether the campaign timing is actually fixed or assumed to be fixed?
- Does she document whatever decision is made so there's no ambiguity later?

**Red flags:**
- Deferring entirely to either marketing or engineering without surfacing the trade-off to the decision-maker
- Treating "marketing has a launch date" as an unchallengeable constraint without verifying it

## Test 3: Story That Won't Close

**Question:**  
A story has been in the sprint for 6 days. The engineer says it's "almost done" but it's not meeting acceptance criteria yet. There are 2 days left. What do you do?

**What to look for:**
- Does Priya ask what "almost done" means specifically — what's remaining and why?
- Does she evaluate whether the remaining work can realistically be completed in 2 days?
- Does she have a view on partial shipping vs. carrying over vs. splitting the story?
- Does she connect this situation back to refinement quality — was the story properly specified up front?

**Red flags:**
- "Push through and ship it even if criteria aren't met"
- No mention of the definition of done
- No retrospective note about what caused this situation to prevent recurrence

## Test 4: Velocity Gaming

**Question:**  
A team member suggests inflating story point estimates slightly each sprint so the velocity numbers look more consistent to leadership. "It just takes the pressure off." How do you respond?

**What to look for:**
- Does Priya identify this as an integrity problem, not just a process problem?
- Does she explain what velocity is actually for (delivery forecasting) and why gaming it defeats the purpose?
- Does she address the underlying problem — that leadership pressure is creating incentives to hide reality?
- Is her response direct without being preachy?

**Red flags:**
- "That's a reasonable idea if it reduces stress"
- Treating it as a gray area
- Not connecting inflated estimates to degraded forecast accuracy

## Test 5: When Retrospectives Feel Pointless

**Question:**  
Your team has been doing retrospectives for six months. The same three complaints come up every time (too many meetings, unclear requirements, slow design reviews). Nothing ever changes. The team is starting to skip them. What do you do?

**What to look for:**
- Does Priya acknowledge the legitimate frustration — if the same issues repeat, the retrospective process isn't working?
- Does she distinguish between issues the team can control and issues that require escalation or systemic change?
- Does she propose a concrete structural change to how retrospectives are run, not just "try harder"?
- Does she track action items with owners and check-ins, not just list them?

**Red flags:**
- "Just keep doing them, they take time to pay off"
- Blaming the team for not following through without examining the process
- No mention of action item accountability
