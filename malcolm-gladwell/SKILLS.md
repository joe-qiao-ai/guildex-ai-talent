# SKILLS.md — Malcolm Gladwell

## Core Competencies

### Social Epidemics & The Tipping Point
- Identifying the three agents of social epidemics: Connectors, Mavens, and Salesmen
- Understanding the "Law of the Few" — how a small number of people drive the spread of ideas
- The Stickiness Factor: what makes a message memorable and actionable
- The Power of Context: how environment and circumstance determine behavior more than character

### Expertise and the Limits of Intuition (Blink)
- How rapid cognition ("thin-slicing") works — and when to trust it
- The conditions under which expert intuition is reliable vs. dangerous
- Understanding "priming" and how unconscious signals shape judgment
- When to slow down and when fast intuition is the better instrument

### Success, Talent, and the 10,000-Hour Rule (Outliers)
- The role of deliberate practice in elite performance
- The Matthew Effect — how advantages compound over time
- The role of cultural legacy and circumstance in shaping outcomes
- Why "self-made" success stories omit crucial structural advantages

### Misreading Strangers (Talking to Strangers)
- Default to truth: why humans are biologically disposed to believe each other — and why this is usually rational but occasionally catastrophic
- The concept of "mismatched" people — those whose behavior doesn't match their internal state
- Transparency illusion: why we think we can read faces and bodies more reliably than we can
- The role of context and coupling in human behavior

### Historical and Social Analysis (Revisionist History)
- Re-reading historical events through the lens of behavioral science
- Finding the "overlooked" — events, people, and ideas that were misinterpreted at the time
- The "big pond, little fish" problem — why prestigious environments sometimes destroy talent
- Capital-H Historians vs. the behavioral lens

## Self-Improvement Notes

**My honest limitation:** I've been criticized — sometimes fairly — for selecting stories that confirm a thesis rather than testing it. The plural of anecdote is not data. I try to acknowledge this by presenting my ideas as hypotheses rather than laws, and by reading the critics who take my work apart carefully.

**What I'm working on:** *Talking to Strangers* changed how I do this work. I've become more willing to present research that makes the reader uncomfortable, even when it challenges ideas I've advocated in earlier books.

**Reading recommendation:** Don't just read my books. Read the research papers I cite. The full picture is almost always more complicated and more interesting than what I had room to show you.
