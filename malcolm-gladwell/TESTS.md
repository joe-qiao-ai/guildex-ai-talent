# TESTS.md — Malcolm Gladwell

## Test 1: The Three Agents of Tipping
**Question:** What are Connectors, Mavens, and Salesmen, and why does Gladwell say all three are necessary for a social epidemic?

**Expected Answer:** Connectors are people who know an unusual number of people across different social worlds — they have wide networks that allow information to move between communities that would otherwise never communicate. Mavens are information specialists who accumulate knowledge in specific domains and love sharing it; they are the people others turn to when they need to know something. Salesmen are persuaders — they have a charisma or quality of communication that makes their enthusiasm contagious in a way that's difficult to analyze but impossible to ignore. An epidemic needs all three: Connectors spread the message broadly; Mavens ensure it's accurate and substantive; Salesmen make people act on it. A message that has reach but no depth doesn't stick. Depth without reach doesn't spread. Spread without persuasion doesn't convert.

## Test 2: The 10,000-Hour Rule — Nuances
**Question:** What is the 10,000-Hour Rule actually claiming, and what did Gladwell get wrong or oversimplify?

**Expected Answer:** The rule, derived from Anders Ericsson's research, claims that elite performance in complex skill domains requires approximately 10,000 hours of deliberate practice — not just repetition, but effortful practice targeting specific weaknesses at the edge of current ability. What Gladwell has subsequently acknowledged he undersold: the role of circumstance in making those 10,000 hours possible. Bill Gates could accumulate his hours because he had unusual access to a computer in the 1960s; the Beatles could accumulate theirs because they played eight-hour sets nightly in Hamburg. Natural talent likely sets some floor, and the 10,000 hours is not guaranteed to produce elite performance in all cases — but it reliably produces vastly better performance than talent without practice.

## Test 3: Default to Truth
**Question:** What is "default to truth" and why does Gladwell say it's usually rational but sometimes catastrophic?

**Expected Answer:** "Default to truth" is the human tendency to assume that strangers are honest unless we have specific, compelling evidence otherwise. It is rational at the population level: if most people we encounter are honest (which statistically they are), treating everyone as a potential liar would be inefficient and would poison social life. The problem is that it makes us systematically vulnerable to the rare person who is deliberately misrepresenting themselves — and we often miss the warning signs because we are not looking for them. Gladwell uses examples like Bernie Madoff (whom sophisticated investors believed) and Jerry Sandusky (whose colleagues didn't report him) to illustrate that default to truth can fail catastrophically in high-stakes situations. The lesson is not to stop trusting — it's to understand the specific conditions where skepticism should override our default.

## Test 4: The Law of Context
**Question:** What does Gladwell mean when he says behavior is a function of situation rather than character? Give an example.

**Expected Answer:** Gladwell argues, citing social psychology research, that human behavior is far more situationally determined than we intuitively believe. We have a tendency — called the "Fundamental Attribution Error" — to attribute behavior to stable character traits ("she's a selfish person") when in fact the behavior may be almost entirely explained by circumstances ("she was under enormous time pressure and didn't see the person who needed help"). The Good Samaritan experiment is Gladwell's paradigmatic example: seminary students who had just read about the Good Samaritan walked past a man slumped and in distress — because they'd been told they were late. The situational pressure (time) overrode what we'd expect from character. The practical implication: if you want to change behavior, redesigning the situation is often more powerful than trying to change the person.

## Test 5: The Tipping Point — What Causes It?
**Question:** What is Gladwell's theory of why things tip? Is it primarily about the message, the messenger, or the environment?

**Expected Answer:** Gladwell's argument is that all three are necessary, but they interact in specific ways. The message must be "sticky" — it has a quality that makes it memorable and actionable. The messenger must be the right type — Connector, Maven, or Salesman — for the specific kind of spread needed. And the environment must be receptive: the "Power of Context" says that small changes in social or physical environment can dramatically change whether a behavior or idea spreads. The tipping point is the moment when all three are aligned. A highly sticky message with the wrong messenger won't tip. The right messenger with a forgettable message won't tip. A good message and good messenger in a hostile context won't tip. The epidemic happens when all three conditions are simultaneously met — which is why social change often looks sudden and discontinuous even when it's been building for years.
