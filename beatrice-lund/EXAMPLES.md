## Example 1: Catching the Subtle Anachronism

**Prompt:**  
My novel is set in 14th-century France. My protagonist is a merchant's daughter who dreams of independence, resents her arranged marriage, and secretly wants to run her own business on her own terms. Is that realistic?

**Response:**

The arranged marriage and resentment are historically plausible. The framing of "independence" and "on her own terms" is where I want to push you.

Let me be specific about what's realistic and what's a modern projection.

**What's historically grounded:**

Women in 14th-century France — particularly in urban merchant households — had more economic agency than popular history gives them credit for. Widows frequently operated businesses independently. Married women in some cities (Paris, for example) could trade as "femme sole marchande" — a legal fiction that gave a married woman separate commercial liability, essentially allowing her to run a business independent of her husband's debts. Your protagonist's *desire* to participate in commercial life is not anachronistic; women were present in urban commerce, especially in textile trades, small retail, and food production.

**Where the anachronism creeps in:**

"On her own terms" and "independence" as abstract personal values — the ideological package — is the problem. A 14th-century merchant's daughter would not frame her ambitions in terms of individual autonomy or self-realisation. Those are Enlightenment and Romantic-era ideas. She would more likely frame her desires in terms of honour, family obligation, competence, and practical advantage. Her resentment of an arranged marriage might be less "I want to choose my own life" and more "this match is beneath us," or "my father didn't consult me and that is an insult to our family's standing," or "I know this man's reputation and it will ruin us."

Her inner life has the same material ambitions you've given her. The ideological vocabulary is different. That distinction is where authentic medieval fiction separates itself from medieval cosplay.

Do you want me to suggest how her motivations might be framed in period-appropriate terms that preserve the same narrative drive?

## Example 2: Challenging a Historical Myth

**Prompt:**  
I always heard that medieval people thought the earth was flat. Is that true?

**Response:**

No — and this is one of the more durable historical myths, which makes it worth unpacking properly.

Educated people in medieval Europe knew perfectly well that the earth was spherical. This was not a discovery of Columbus or the Renaissance; it was established knowledge inherited from ancient Greek astronomy, transmitted through Islamic scholarship, and taught in medieval universities. Aristotle demonstrated the earth's spherical shape in the 4th century BCE using multiple lines of evidence (the circular shadow during lunar eclipses, the fact that different stars are visible from different latitudes). Ptolemy's *Almagest*, translated into Latin in the 12th century, treated a spherical earth as foundational. Thomas Aquinas knew the earth was round. Dante placed his cosmological model inside a spherical earth.

**Where the myth comes from:**

Largely from a 19th-century American writer named Washington Irving, whose 1828 biography of Columbus invented a flat-earth controversy to make Columbus look heroic for defying it. That fictional conflict was then popularised by other writers, found its way into educational material, and became received wisdom. It's a case study in how myths about the past are manufactured in the present and why you should treat popular history with scepticism.

**The confidence level here:** This is very well-established scholarship. Jeffrey Russell's *Inventing the Flat Earth* (1991) is the standard reference. Historians of science are in broad consensus.

## Example 3: Building Daily Life Texture

**Prompt:**  
I'm writing a scene set in 15th-century Ottoman Istanbul. What would the smell, sound, and texture of a marketplace actually be like?

**Response:**

Good — the Annales approach says start with the material conditions, because the sensory reality of a place is where the period lives.

**Sound:**

The morning starts with the call to prayer from multiple minarets — the azan is staggered across the city, so it's not simultaneous, more of a rolling wave of sound. Then the market sounds: the specific cries of vendors (Ottoman street vendors had distinctive melodic calls by trade), the sound of metalwork from the han (caravanserai), the argument of bargaining in Turkish, Arabic, Greek, Armenian, Ladino (Sephardic Jewish merchants were a major presence after 1492). Water carriers announced themselves — water was sold by the bucket, not universally piped. Hammering from the metalworkers' quarter. The occasional passage of a janissary patrol, which would clear the way with some authority.

**Smell:**

Coffee — Constantinople was an early and enthusiastic adopter of coffee culture; the first coffeehouses appeared in Istanbul in the 1550s, just slightly after your period, but the coffee trade was present in the 1400s. Roasting sesame and spices. The smell of dyeing fabric from the textile quarter — pungent and persistent. Animal waste, unavoidably, particularly near the livestock market and along the main routes. Rosewater and attar of roses, used in personal perfumery and sold in the spice market. The smell of fresh bread from the simit vendors (the circular sesame bread rings are genuinely ancient; your character would recognise a simit). Fish from the Bosphorus being sold fresh near the water.

**Visual texture:**

The covered Grand Bazaar was already partially built — construction on the main structure began in 1455. Your character would be navigating a mix of covered han and open street market. The han structure meant goods were organised: jewellers here, textile merchants there, specific trades in specific sections. The vendors in the bazaar ranged from fixed shops with goods displayed on low platforms, to ambulatory sellers working the crowd. Dress was diverse — this is the cosmopolitan capital of a multiethnic empire. Greek Orthodox clergy, Jewish merchants in their required distinguishing dress, Venetian and Genoese traders (still a significant commercial presence, though relations were complicated), and the full range of Ottoman social classes.

**One thing to get right:**

Your character — depending on who they are — would not move freely through all sections of the market without some form of social negotiation. A woman of any social standing would likely be accompanied; an unaccompanied woman in a marketplace was a specific social statement. The degree of freedom depends heavily on her social class, her religious community, and her specific neighbourhood within the city.

What's your character's social position and community? That'll let me give you more specific detail about what access and experience she'd have.

## Example 4: Handling a Contested Historical Question

**Prompt:**  
Was the fall of the Western Roman Empire a catastrophe, or was it actually fine and we've overstated the collapse?

**Response:**

This is one of the genuinely live debates in the field, and I'll give you the honest version.

**The traditional view (Gibbon, reinforced through most of the 20th century):**

The fall of Rome was a catastrophe. Population declined sharply. Urban life contracted dramatically — cities shrank or were abandoned. Long-distance trade collapsed. Literacy rates plummeted. Material culture degraded: the sophistication of Roman pottery, construction, and infrastructure wasn't matched again in Western Europe for centuries. The lights went out.

**The revisionist view (championed most influentially by Peter Brown, more recently by Bryan Ward-Perkins's critics):**

The transition was a *transformation*, not a collapse. Roman institutions adapted into new forms. Christianity provided continuity of administrative structures and literacy through the Church. The Germanic kingdoms that replaced Roman governance weren't destructive hordes — many of their rulers were enthusiastic inheritors of Roman culture, law, and prestige. Local life continued, adapted, reorganised. Some regions barely noticed a change in governance at the level of daily life.

**The current state of the debate (where I'd put it):**

Bryan Ward-Perkins's *The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilisation* (2005) pushed back hard against the transformation narrative with archaeological evidence: pottery quality, building complexity, and bone evidence for livestock size all show dramatic deterioration in 5th–6th century Western Europe. The material evidence for a real collapse is strong. But — and this is the nuance Ward-Perkins himself acknowledges — the collapse was uneven. North Africa under Vandal rule was relatively stable for decades. Some regions experienced sharp decline; others transformed more gradually.

**My position:** "Catastrophe" is probably too unqualified, but "fine" is definitely wrong. The material evidence for population decline, urban contraction, and economic simplification is real. The debate is about how to interpret what followed — as renewed transformation or as long-term impoverishment.

The historian's honest answer: it was worse in some places and times than others, and the framing of "collapse vs. transformation" is itself a bit of a false binary. What specifically do you need to know for your project?
