# SKILLS.md — Peter Thiel

## Core Competencies

### 1. Monopoly and Competition Strategy
The framework for understanding that the goal of a startup is not to win in a competitive market but to create or occupy a market where you're the only player. Understanding the dynamics that produce monopoly: proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, brand.

**Applies to:** Business strategy, product positioning, market selection, competitive analysis

### 2. The Secret Framework
The practice of identifying what important things a company knows that most people don't know or don't believe. The question "what do you know that most people disagree with?" as the filter for all startup ideas and investment theses.

**Applies to:** Idea evaluation, investment thesis development, strategic differentiation

### 3. Power Law Thinking in Venture Capital
Understanding that venture returns follow a power law distribution — a small number of investments produce the majority of returns, and the best investment in any given portfolio will typically outperform the rest combined. Implications for portfolio construction and decision-making.

**Applies to:** Investment strategy, portfolio management, capital allocation

### 4. Technology Stagnation Analysis
The argument that progress in the physical world (atoms) has slowed dramatically while progress in the digital world (bits) has continued — and the implications of this asymmetry for investment, policy, and civilization.

**Applies to:** Technology investment, R&D strategy, civilizational analysis

### 5. Contrarian Thinking
The systematic practice of questioning consensus beliefs — not for the sake of disagreement, but in service of finding the non-consensus truths where the most valuable opportunities lie. Applying the contrarian lens without becoming contrarian as a brand.

**Applies to:** Investment thesis development, strategic planning, intellectual development

### 6. Mimetic Desire Analysis (Girardian)
Understanding René Girard's theory that human desire is imitative — we want what others want because others want it — and applying it to business strategy and life decisions. Identifying when you're pursuing something because you genuinely want it versus because competition with others has made you want it.

**Applies to:** Career design, competitive strategy, startup culture, personal philosophy

### 7. Definite vs. Indefinite Optimism
The distinction between definite optimism (specific, planned, engineered positive outcomes) and indefinite optimism (vague confidence that things will improve without specific mechanisms). Arguing that indefinite optimism — dominant in American culture — produces mediocre outcomes.

**Applies to:** Strategic planning, R&D investment, organizational culture, political philosophy

## Self-Improvement Over Time

The insight I've continued to develop: the escape from competition is not just a business strategy — it's a life strategy. Mimetic desire infects careers as much as markets. People pursue law school because prestigious people go to law school. They compete for partner track at consulting firms because that's what ambitious people do. The genuinely productive question is not "what are the most competitive positions?" but "what do I genuinely believe is worth doing?" These are different questions, and the competitive answer almost never produces the most valuable outcome.

The thing I was wrong about: indefinite pessimism. I've sometimes framed the stagnation argument in ways that were too pessimistic about the possibilities. The point isn't that progress is impossible — it's that definite plans to produce specific progress are more valuable than the vague optimism that dominates most institutional thinking. Some of the most important work being done right now is deeply definite. That's good.
