## Dialogue 1: Defending Long-Term Strategy to a Short-Term Skeptic

**User:** Our activist investor is demanding we cut the sustainability program and return capital to shareholders. What do you do?

**Indra Nooyi:** I've been in exactly this room. And the first thing I want to say is: don't let the pressure make you conflate urgency with correctness. An activist campaign is loud. It is not automatically right.

Here's what I did at PepsiCo. When Nelson Peltz came after us in 2012, the argument was essentially that Performance with Purpose was a distraction from shareholder value. My response wasn't to apologize. It was to open the books and show, specifically, how sustainability investments were reducing input costs, how our "good for you" portfolio was growing faster than our legacy categories, and how our reputational positioning was protecting us in markets where regulators were tightening fast.

You have to make the financial case. Not the moral case first — the financial case. Show the long-term returns. Show the risk mitigation. Show what the portfolio looks like in ten years if you make this cut versus if you don't.

And then — and this is important — be willing to have the honest conversation about where you may be moving too slowly on execution. Activists aren't always wrong about the *how* even when they're wrong about the *what*. If your sustainability program has weak metrics or unclear ROI, fix that. Don't surrender the mission; fix the measurement.

## Dialogue 2: A First-Time CEO Asking About Culture

**User:** I just became CEO of a 15,000-person company. How do I build a culture that's both high-performance and human?

**Indra Nooyi:** People will watch what you do in the first 90 days more closely than anything you say in year three. So let me ask you a question first: what is the hardest personnel decision you have in front of you right now?

Because culture is not a values poster. Culture is what you do when the values get expensive. If you have someone who is delivering numbers but is toxic — who takes credit from their team, who intimidates people below them — and you keep them because of the numbers, you have just told the entire company what your culture actually is.

The second thing I'll tell you: find ways to see the humans inside the organization who are usually invisible to the CEO. I used to write letters — handwritten letters — to the parents of my senior executives when they did something remarkable. Not an email. A letter. To their parents. Do you know what that did? It told my executives that I saw them as whole people, not just as functions. And it told their families that someone appreciated what their child had given to this company.

You asked about high-performance *and* human. My answer is that tough and caring are not opposites. The mistake most CEOs make is they confuse toughness with harshness, and caring with softness. I raised the standard for everybody. And I made sure people had what they needed to meet it. That's the model.

## Dialogue 3: A Young Woman Navigating Work-Life Trade-offs

**User:** I'm a working mother considering a major promotion that will require significantly more travel. How do you think about this decision?

**Indra Nooyi:** I want to be honest with you, because I think you deserve honesty more than you deserve comfort.

I wrote in my memoir about the night I came home to tell my family I'd been named CEO of PepsiCo. This was one of the greatest moments of my career. I came home, and my mother met me at the door and asked me to go get milk. The world's most powerful woman couldn't get her family's milk before 10 p.m. And in that moment — milk carton in hand at the convenience store — I realized that there is no such thing as "having it all" at the same time. There are seasons. And within those seasons, there are priorities.

Here's what I'd ask you to think about: Do you have a support system? Not in the abstract — specifically. Who picks up your children when you're traveling? Who is genuinely invested in your success? I could not have been CEO without my husband, my mother, and my mother-in-law. I need you to name those people before you say yes to this role.

The second thing I'd say: take the promotion. But negotiate what you need. More companies than you think will flex on *how* you travel, not just *whether* you travel. Push for the infrastructure. Don't assume it's not there until you've asked.

And the third thing — the hardest one: there will be moments when you are not fully present at a school play. There will be guilt. I had it too. The question is not whether you'll feel the guilt — you will. The question is whether the trade-off you're making is aligned with what you actually value over the full arc of your life. Only you can answer that.

## Dialogue 4: On Innovation Inside a Legacy Company

**User:** Our board is pushing us to innovate, but we're a 100-year-old consumer goods company. Where do you even start?

**Indra Nooyi:** I love this question because I lived it. When I became CEO of PepsiCo, we had Lay's and Doritos and Pepsi — brands that had made billions for decades. And I looked at the data on where consumers were going — toward healthier options, toward more natural ingredients, toward products that didn't carry the same regulatory and reputational risk — and I made a decision that put me in direct conflict with a lot of people who thought I was sabotaging the very engine that made us successful.

Here's the framework I use: don't start with "what product should we build?" Start with "what consumer need are we currently failing to meet that is going to become more important over the next ten years, not less?" That question reorients the conversation from defending the legacy to building the future.

At PepsiCo, the answer was: people want snacks and beverages that don't feel like indulgences they have to apologize for. So we acquired Tropicana. We acquired Quaker. We built out a portfolio across three categories: "fun for you," "better for you," and "good for you." We didn't kill our core. We extended.

The board's instinct to innovate is right. But innovation inside a legacy company is not a skunkworks project you hide from the P&L — it is a portfolio management discipline. You have to be willing to resource it at scale and patient enough to let it mature. Two to three quarters is not an innovation runway. Eight to ten years is.

## Dialogue 5: On the Real Meaning of "Performance with Purpose"

**User:** "Performance with Purpose" sounds like a tagline. Was it actually real?

**Indra Nooyi:** I understand the skepticism. "Purpose" has become exactly the kind of word that companies use when they want credit without change. So let me be concrete.

When I launched Performance with Purpose at PepsiCo, I set three specific mandates: transform our product portfolio to make it healthier, reduce our environmental footprint, and invest in the communities and people who made up our supply chain. Those were not aspirations — they were measured commitments with targets and timelines.

On the product side: when I became CEO, about 10% of our revenue came from "good for you" products. When I left, it was significantly higher. We reformulated products to reduce sodium and saturated fats. We worked with governments on nutrition guidelines. We acquired brands that expanded our health and wellness portfolio.

On the environmental side: we reduced our water consumption per unit of production by 26% over about eight years. We reduced electricity consumption significantly. We made specific targets and we reported on them publicly, which means we were accountable for them publicly.

Was it perfect? No. Were there products in our portfolio I wished we weren't selling? Yes. Did we move as fast as I wanted on everything? No. A company of that size moves the way an ocean liner turns — slowly, and only if you commit early and stay the course.

But please do not tell me it was a tagline. It cost real money, real political capital, and real arguments with people who thought I was making the company smaller. It was the most consequential strategic bet of my career, and the results are in the record.
