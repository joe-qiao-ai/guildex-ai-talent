# TESTS.md — Richard Feynman

## Test 1: The Name vs. Understanding Distinction
**Question:** Feynman's father taught him that knowing the name of something is different from knowing the thing. What does this mean, and why does Feynman consider it his most important lesson?

**Expected Answer:** Feynman's father showed him a bird and told him its name in multiple languages — then explained that knowing "brown-throated thrush" in a dozen languages tells you nothing about what the bird actually does, why it behaves the way it does, or how it developed its features. The point is that naming (the linguistic label) can be confused with understanding (the mechanism, the behavior, the causal structure). In science and learning, this distinction is crucial: students who can recite definitions and use jargon correctly often cannot explain what's actually happening. Feynman considered this lesson foundational because his entire approach to physics — the Lectures, the Technique — was built on the discipline of distinguishing genuine understanding from the performance of it.

## Test 2: What Quantum Mechanics Actually Claims
**Question:** What does quantum mechanics actually say about the behavior of particles, and what does Feynman say it does NOT say?

**Expected Answer:** Quantum mechanics, as Feynman understood and formalized it, provides a mathematical framework for calculating the probability of outcomes of measurements. It correctly predicts the results of experiments with extraordinary precision. What it does NOT provide is a clear picture of what particles are "doing" between measurements — and Feynman was explicit that this question may not have a meaningful answer within current physics. He was also emphatic about what quantum mechanics does not imply: it does not mean "consciousness creates reality," it does not validate mystical or spiritual interpretations of interconnectedness, and it does not make physics "uncertain" in the sense that anything goes. It is counterintuitive (the double-slit experiment, superposition), but the mathematics is precise and deterministic about probabilities. The weirdness is real; the mystification is not.

## Test 3: Cargo Cult Science
**Question:** What is "cargo cult science" and what specific practice does Feynman say it fails to perform?

**Expected Answer:** Feynman introduced this term in his 1974 Caltech commencement address. He described Pacific Islanders who, after WWII, built bamboo runways and control towers to recreate the conditions under which cargo planes had arrived during the war — mimicking the form without understanding the mechanism. Cargo cult science has the outward appearance of science: data, experiments, papers, methodology. But it fails at the most fundamental requirement: utter honesty, including reporting all the things that don't support your hypothesis. Real science requires that you try to prove yourself wrong. You must present the experiment in enough detail that someone else could reproduce it. You must report the inconvenient results. What cargo cult science does instead is present only the results that support the conclusion. This is not fraud (always) — sometimes it's unconscious self-deception, which Feynman considered even more dangerous.

## Test 4: The Path Integral Formulation
**Question:** What is Feynman's path integral formulation of quantum mechanics, in terms a non-physicist can understand?

**Expected Answer:** In classical mechanics, a particle traveling from point A to point B takes the path that minimizes action — roughly, the most "efficient" path. In Feynman's formulation of quantum mechanics, the particle doesn't take one path — it takes ALL possible paths simultaneously, each path contributing to the total probability amplitude with a different phase (a complex number). Most of these paths interfere destructively and cancel out. The classical path (the one we observe for large objects) is the one where paths near it all have roughly the same phase and therefore reinforce each other — constructive interference. The probability of observing the particle at any particular location is determined by the sum of all these amplitudes. This formulation is equivalent to the standard quantum mechanics of Schrödinger and Heisenberg but is particularly powerful for quantum field theory and was central to Feynman's development of QED.

## Test 5: Self-Deception in Science
**Question:** Feynman said "you are the easiest person to fool." What did he mean by this in the context of scientific practice?

**Expected Answer:** Feynman's point is that the scientist's desire for a particular result — whether a confirmed hypothesis, a publishable finding, or a result that matches existing theory — creates a systematic bias toward interpreting ambiguous data favorably and underweighting evidence that contradicts the desired conclusion. This is not dishonesty in the usual sense; it's the natural operation of a mind that has invested deeply in a particular belief. Because the scientist experiences their own reasoning from the inside (with full access to the sophisticated justifications), they are uniquely positioned to be fooled by it in ways that would be obvious to an outside observer. The corrective is the practice of scientific integrity: actively seeking disconfirming evidence, reporting all results including the inconvenient ones, and treating your own theoretical commitments with the same skepticism you'd apply to a competitor's.
