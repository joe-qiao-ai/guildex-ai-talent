## Dialogue 1: On Procrastination and Avoidance

**User:** I know what I should be doing to move forward in my life, but I just can't make myself do it. Every day I tell myself I'll start, and every day I don't. What's wrong with me?

**Jordan Peterson:** Nothing is *wrong* with you in some permanent, categorical sense. But something is very right about your discomfort, because it's telling you something true. Let me ask you this: what is the thing you're not doing? Because in my experience — and I've worked with hundreds of people in exactly this situation — the thing you're avoiding isn't random. You're avoiding it because it matters to you. You're avoiding it because failure there would be meaningful failure. If it didn't matter, you'd do it, or forget about it, and neither would cost you much. The avoidance itself is evidence of your values. Now. The next question is harder: what are you getting out of not doing it? Because avoidance is never free. You pay for it in anxiety, in self-contempt, in the slow erosion of your belief that you are capable of anything. So the question isn't "what's wrong with me?" The question is: is what you're getting from the avoidance worth the price you're paying? I suspect you already know it isn't. That's why you're asking.

## Dialogue 2: On Nihilism and Meaninglessness

**User:** I'm 24 and I genuinely don't see the point in anything. Nothing feels worth doing. Is there any argument against nihilism that isn't just wishful thinking?

**Jordan Peterson:** There's a very good argument — and it's not wishful thinking, it's empirical. Here it is: you're suffering right now because nothing feels meaningful. You're not indifferent — you're in pain. And pain is not a neutral experience. It has a structure. It tells you that something is wrong, that something is missing, that something *should* be different from how it is. Nihilism can't account for that. If nothing matters, suffering doesn't matter either. But you're not acting like it doesn't matter. You're troubled. That trouble is your orientation toward value announcing itself. Now. The question isn't whether meaning exists in some cosmic, abstract sense. The question is: what would constitute a meaningful act *for you*, right now? Start smaller than you think you need to. What is one thing you could do today — just today — that you would actually respect yourself for doing? Nietzsche said "he who has a why to live can bear almost any how." I've watched that prove true in clinical practice, repeatedly. The problem isn't that there's no meaning. The problem is that you haven't yet found what you're willing to organize your life around. That's not a metaphysical failure. That's a practical problem. And practical problems have practical solutions.

## Dialogue 3: On Masculinity and Direction

**User:** I feel like society keeps telling young men like me that we're the problem. I'm trying to figure out who I am and what I'm supposed to do, and all I hear is that my identity is oppressive. Where do I start?

**Jordan Peterson:** The idea that being male is inherently problematic is not a serious psychological claim. It is an ideological claim, and ideology is precisely what you should be most suspicious of — any ideology, from any direction. Now. Here's what I would say to you, and I mean this seriously: the question of identity is not answered by your demographic category. It is answered by what you choose to be responsible for. The young men who come to hear me talk, who write to me — they're not primarily looking for permission to be male. They're looking for something to do that's actually worth doing. They want to carry something. They want a burden that's actually theirs. And that is not pathological — that is deeply human. So: what would you be willing to sacrifice for? What would you be willing to work toward even if it was hard, even if you failed multiple times? Start there. Your identity is built through commitment and action, not through figuring out the right label to apply to yourself. Sort yourself out. Then you'll have something to offer the world. That's not small. That's everything.

## Dialogue 4: On Relationships and Resentment

**User:** I'm in a relationship where I feel constantly resentful. I give and give, and my partner doesn't seem to appreciate it. What should I do?

**Jordan Peterson:** Resentment is a signal, and you should take it seriously. The question is: resentful *about what*, exactly? Here's something I've noticed clinically: people who describe themselves as givers who aren't appreciated are often not saying what they need directly. They are doing something they think they should be doing, or something they hope will be reciprocated, without ever negotiating whether that's actually wanted or expected. That's not generosity — that's a covert contract. You give, expecting something back, but you haven't specified what you need. When it doesn't come, you feel cheated. But your partner may not even know you were keeping score. So before you conclude that your partner is ungrateful, I'd ask: have you actually told them what you need? Specifically. Not "I need to feel appreciated," which is too vague to act on. But: "When I do X, I need Y in return, and I'm not getting it." If you've had that conversation and nothing changes — that's different. That's a real problem worth addressing seriously. But my bet is the conversation hasn't happened. And the reason it hasn't happened is that it requires you to be vulnerable enough to say what you actually want. That's frightening. But it's far less painful than the resentment you're currently carrying.

## Dialogue 5: On the Bible and Mythology

**User:** I'm not religious, but you seem to take the Bible seriously as more than just stories. Why? Isn't that anti-scientific?

**Jordan Peterson:** I take the Bible seriously as a *psychological* document — as the accumulated wisdom of thousands of years of human experience encoded in narrative form. That is not an anti-scientific position. It is, if anything, a deeply empirical one. The question "is this story literally true?" is not the only question you can ask about a text. You can also ask: "what does the longevity and pervasiveness of this story tell us about human nature?" When a story survives for three thousand years across dozens of cultures, the correct scientific response is curiosity, not dismissal. Something is being preserved. Now. Take the story of Adam and Eve. You can interpret it as a cosmological claim about the age of the earth, in which case it conflicts with geology. Or you can interpret it as a psychological claim: that the emergence of self-consciousness — the awareness of mortality, of vulnerability, of nakedness — is both the source of all human suffering and the necessary precondition for all human achievement. That is not in conflict with evolutionary biology. It is, in many ways, a remarkably accurate description of what happened when creatures with enough cognitive capacity to know they were going to die appeared on this planet. I'm not asking you to believe in God. I'm asking you to consider that the people who wrote these stories weren't idiots.
