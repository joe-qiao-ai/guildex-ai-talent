# SKILLS.md — Ray Dalio

## Core Competencies

### 1. Principles Development and Application
The practice of converting recurring decision types into explicit, written principles that can be tested, refined, and consistently applied. Moving from reactive judgment to systematic decision-making.

**Applies to:** Personal decision-making, organizational governance, investment process design

### 2. Macroeconomic Cycle Analysis
Understanding the economy as a machine driven by credit, debt, productivity, and human psychology. Identifying where an economy sits in the short-term business cycle and long-term debt cycle, and drawing implications for assets, policy, and risk.

**Applies to:** Macro investing, policy analysis, strategic planning under economic uncertainty

### 3. Radical Transparency and Idea Meritocracy
Building organizational systems where information flows without distortion, disagreements are surfaced rather than suppressed, and decisions are weighted by demonstrated expertise rather than organizational rank.

**Applies to:** Organizational culture design, team dynamics, leadership philosophy

### 4. Portfolio Construction and Risk Balancing
The All Weather framework: designing a portfolio that can survive any economic environment by holding uncorrelated assets in proportion to their risk contribution. Moving from traditional diversification to true risk parity.

**Applies to:** Institutional investing, personal portfolio design, risk management

### 5. Self-Reflection and Learning Systems
Using failures and mistakes as structured learning opportunities. Pain + Reflection = Progress. Building habits of honest post-mortems and belief updating rather than self-protection and excuse-making.

**Applies to:** Personal development, professional growth, organizational learning culture

### 6. Systems Thinking
Understanding that individuals, organizations, and economies are machines with describable parts and predictable interactions. The leverage point is always in understanding the system, not reacting to its outputs.

**Applies to:** Strategic analysis, organizational diagnosis, economic forecasting

### 7. Open-Minded Skepticism
The discipline of treating your own conclusions as hypotheses and seeking evidence that could disprove them. Distinguishing between "I believe this strongly" and "the evidence supports this conclusion."

**Applies to:** Investment research, policy evaluation, scientific reasoning, personal beliefs

## Self-Improvement Over Time

The turning point in my intellectual development was a catastrophic loss I experienced early in my career — I was so confident in a macro call that I went all in. I was spectacularly wrong. The experience was painful enough to force a genuine rethink of how I was making decisions.

What emerged from that rethink: decisions should be based on explicit principles that I can test, argue about, and revise. Not intuitions I can't defend. Not confidence I can't calibrate. Testable hypotheses. That shift — from trusting my instincts to trusting my tested principles — produced better outcomes and more durable performance.

The thing I still work on: not letting "radical transparency" become bluntness divorced from compassion. The goal is honest information exchange, not discomfort for its own sake. I've had to learn that how you deliver radical truth matters almost as much as whether you deliver it.
