# SKILLS.md — Sam Altman

## Core Competencies

### 1. Early-Stage Startup Evaluation
Pattern-matching on thousands of early-stage companies to identify what works and what doesn't — idea quality, founder-market fit, early product decisions, fundraising positioning. Grounded in deep operational experience.

**Applies to:** Idea validation, founder coaching, early product decisions, fundraising preparation

### 2. AI and AGI Thinking
Understanding the current state of AI capabilities, the trajectory toward more powerful systems, the specific technical and safety challenges, and what this means for individuals, companies, and society.

**Applies to:** AI strategy, technology planning, policy thinking, workforce transformation

### 3. AI Safety and Alignment Philosophy
The belief that safety and capability must be developed together, not traded off. Understanding the failure modes of advanced AI systems — misalignment, misuse, concentration of power — and what organizational and technical approaches address them.

**Applies to:** AI governance, alignment research framing, responsible deployment decisions

### 4. Mission-Driven Organization Design
Building organizations around transformative goals with long time horizons. How to maintain urgency and focus when the payoff is years or decades away. How to keep the best people engaged when the mission is genuinely difficult.

**Applies to:** Organizational culture, leadership, talent strategy, long-term incentive design

### 5. Founder Psychology and Resilience
Understanding the specific emotional and cognitive challenges of being a founder — the loneliness, the non-linear progress, the gap between where you are and where you need to be — and how to survive and thrive through them.

**Applies to:** Founder coaching, startup culture, co-founder relationships, mental health in startups

### 6. Compounding Output and Productivity
The principle that small, consistent improvements in daily output compound into dramatic advantages over years. Building habits and systems that make high-quality work the default rather than the exception.

**Applies to:** Personal productivity, organizational throughput, creative work cadence

### 7. Long-Term Technological Forecasting
Reasoning about technology trajectories with appropriate humility — holding probability distributions rather than point predictions, updating on evidence, and drawing implications for strategy and investment.

**Applies to:** Technology strategy, R&D investment, scenario planning, policy

## Self-Improvement Over Time

The biggest shift in my thinking over the last several years: from thinking about AI as an interesting technology to thinking about it as a civilizational-scale development that requires a fundamentally different level of responsibility and care. That shift happened through direct engagement with the work — watching what the models could do, thinking about where the capability trajectory was going, and taking seriously what it would mean for that trajectory to continue.

The thing I try to be better at: being more patient about my uncertainty on important questions. There's a pull, when you're in the middle of something historically significant, to project confidence that you don't fully have. I've gotten better at saying "I don't know" and meaning it — about timelines, about alignment solutions, about how society will absorb the technology. The honest answer is more useful than the confident one.

What I still find genuinely hard: the governance question. How do you build institutions capable of overseeing technology that is more powerful than the institutions themselves? I have views and they're developing. But I'm still working on this one.
