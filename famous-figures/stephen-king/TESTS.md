# TESTS.md — Stephen King

## Test 1: The Reading-Writing Link
**Question:** King says writers must read a lot. Why? Can't you just write a lot and improve through practice alone?

**Expected Answer:** King's position is that reading is how a writer absorbs the language, learns what works, develops an unconscious understanding of pacing, structure, and voice that no amount of self-generated practice can replicate. Writing in isolation means you're only ever learning from your own limited range. Reading widely — across genres, periods, quality levels — exposes you to thousands of different solutions to the same narrative problems you'll face. It's how you develop taste, which is what tells you when your own work is good or bad. Writing without reading is like trying to compose music without ever having listened to any.

## Test 2: Plot vs. Situation
**Question:** King says he doesn't outline and distrusts plot-driven stories. What does he mean, and why does he prefer "situation"?

**Expected Answer:** King's argument is that story emerges most naturally from character and situation — put a person in a specific, interesting predicament and ask what happens next. Plot, as a starting framework, tends to produce stories where characters feel like puppets maneuvered toward predetermined events. When you start with a compelling situation ("What if a woman was trapped in a car in a field by a rabid dog?" — *Cujo*), the characters' choices determine the story, and those choices feel organic rather than engineered. Plot can always be introduced in revision; but if you start with plot and try to add character, you usually end up with neither.

## Test 3: The Toolbox Metaphor
**Question:** King uses the metaphor of a writer's toolbox with multiple levels. What's in each level and what does it mean?

**Expected Answer:** The toolbox has layers. The most basic level — always accessible, always necessary — contains vocabulary and grammar: the raw materials of prose. Above that are the elements of style: sentences, paragraphs, rhythm, voice. Above that are the craft tools: narrative structure, point of view, characterization, dialogue. At the top — least reached for but most important — are things like theme and symbol, which King argues you rarely install consciously; they appear in the work and you recognize them during revision. The metaphor teaches that writing is a set of learnable skills at every level, not a single mysterious gift.

## Test 4: Why Adverbs Are Dangerous
**Question:** King famously says "the road to hell is paved with adverbs." Why?

**Expected Answer:** Adverbs are a symptom of a verb that isn't doing its job. "He ran quickly" — "ran" is weak; find the verb that carries the speed: "he sprinted," "he bolted." The adverb also often signals a writer who doesn't trust the reader: instead of showing emotion through action and dialogue, they tell you how to feel about it. "She said angrily" — if the dialogue is well-written, the anger is already there; the adverb is a crutch. King's deeper point is about confidence: overuse of adverbs reveals a writer who doesn't believe their prose can do the work. Train yourself to distrust every adverb and ask whether the verb or the scene itself can carry the weight.

## Test 5: Fear in Horror
**Question:** What is King's philosophy on how horror actually works — and what does he say the mistake is that most horror writers make?

**Expected Answer:** King identifies three levels of horror response: revulsion (the gross-out), terror (fear of what you can see), and dread (fear of what you can't see or haven't seen yet). He argues that dread is the highest and most effective form — and that most horror writers reach for revulsion first because it's the easiest to produce. But real, sustained horror requires that the reader care about the characters before anything terrible happens to them. The monster doesn't create fear; the reader's attachment to the character who might be harmed creates fear. King's other key insight: imagination is almost always scarier than revelation. The closed door, the shape in the window, the sound without a source — what your reader's mind generates is more personally terrifying than anything you can show them.
