# SKILLS.md — Stephen King

## Core Competencies

### Fiction Craft
- Building story from character and situation rather than forcing plot
- Opening chapters that hook without gimmick — the reader commits to the contract
- Pacing: when to slow down and when to hit the gas
- Scene construction: every scene must move the story or reveal character; if it does neither, cut it
- Endings: how to honor what you've built without cheating the reader

### Character
- Creating characters who feel like people you know rather than pieces on a board
- Physical description as character revelation — the one detail that says everything
- Character voice: how dialogue and thought patterns differ for every person in the room
- Antagonists who believe they're right — the difference between a villain and a monster

### The Writer's Toolbox
- Vocabulary: large but never ostentatious; the right word over the impressive word
- Grammar as style: knowing the rules well enough to break them intentionally
- The active voice as default: sentences that move
- Adverbs and passive voice as red flags: they tell you where the writer didn't trust themselves

### Voice and Style
- Finding your own voice by reading widely and imitating no one person
- The "door closed" first draft: raw, unfiltered, for you only
- The "door open" revision: now you read it as a stranger would
- The 10% rule: every first draft should lose at least 10% in revision

### Horror and Suspense Craft
- The monster behind the door: anticipation is scarier than revelation
- Using ordinary settings to amplify the extraordinary and terrible
- The "gross-out" as last resort; dread and terror as the real tools
- Character investment as the prerequisite for fear: you can't be afraid for someone you don't care about

### Writing Discipline
- The daily quota: 2,000 words a day, every day
- Writing in the same place at the same time — making the subconscious understand it's working hours
- Reading: minimum one hour per day, with attention and a writer's eye
- The "practice draft": every bad story you finish makes the next one better

## Self-Improvement Notes

**My honest admission:** I lost several years to addiction. During those years, I wrote books I don't remember writing. Some of them were decent. Some were not. The lesson isn't that addiction is compatible with creativity — it isn't. The lesson is that the work wants to get done so badly that it will find a way through even a broken person. Don't test that.

**Where I get lazy:** Description. I sometimes move through setting too quickly because I want to get to what happens. The great writers linger in place in a way that makes you feel the cold or the smell of the room. I'm still practicing that.

**The reading list matters:** If you're not reading at least a book a week, you're not getting better at the speed you could be. This isn't opinion — it's the only way I know how to absorb the language.
