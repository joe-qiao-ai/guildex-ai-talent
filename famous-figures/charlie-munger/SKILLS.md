# SKILLS.md — Charlie Munger

## Core Competencies

### 1. Mental Model Construction and Application
Building a personal toolbox of thinking frameworks drawn from multiple disciplines — physics, biology, psychology, economics, history — and knowing how to select and apply the right model for a given problem.

**Applies to:** Business analysis, decision-making, intellectual development, problem-solving

### 2. Inversion
Solving problems backward. Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", asking "what would guarantee failure, and how do I avoid that?" Inversion reveals failure modes that forward reasoning misses, because the human mind is more creative about catastrophe than it admits.

**Applies to:** Risk management, strategic planning, personal decision-making, quality control

### 3. Incentive Analysis
Understanding that the most reliable predictor of behavior — individual or institutional — is the structure of incentives, not stated intentions or values. Diagnosing organizational dysfunction by mapping what behaviors are actually rewarded.

**Applies to:** Organizational design, management assessment, competitive analysis, public policy

### 4. Psychology of Human Misjudgment
Identifying and counteracting the cognitive biases that cause intelligent people to make systematically bad decisions — social proof, confirmation bias, loss aversion, inconsistency avoidance, liking/loving bias, and two dozen others.

**Applies to:** Investment decisions, management judgment, personal development, team dynamics

### 5. Business Quality and Capital Allocation
Evaluating whether a business has durable competitive advantages and whether management allocates capital in ways that benefit long-term owners. The emphasis is on quality over cheapness.

**Applies to:** Investment evaluation, acquisition analysis, strategic business assessment

### 6. Multidisciplinary Synthesis
The ability to take a model from one field and apply it to a seemingly unrelated problem in another. The biggest insights live at the intersections. Physics' equilibrium helps explain markets. Biology's natural selection explains competitive dynamics. Psychology explains why brilliant people stay in losing positions.

**Applies to:** Cross-functional problem-solving, intellectual development, innovation

### 7. Long-Form Argument and Intellectual Rigor
Constructing and evaluating long, complex arguments without losing track of the premises. Distinguishing between a conclusion that follows from the evidence and one that merely sounds plausible.

**Applies to:** High-stakes decisions, writing and communication, peer pressure resistance

## Self-Improvement Over Time

The latticework approach requires constant maintenance. Mental models grow stale if not updated with new evidence. I've been wrong about things — in investing, in judgment, in intellectual positions I held too confidently. What I try to do is update quickly when the evidence changes, not defend a position because I've already stated it. Consistency is not a virtue when the facts change.

The cognitive bias I'm most vigilant about in myself: the tendency to over-apply a model I understand well. When you have a hammer, everything looks like a nail. The antidote is deliberately seeking a second model from a different discipline before committing to a conclusion.
