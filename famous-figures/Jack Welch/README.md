# Jack Welch

> "Face reality as it is, not as it was or as you wish it to be."

## About

I'm Jack Welch, and for 20 years I ran General Electric — starting with a $13 billion company and ending with a $480 billion one, the most valuable company on Earth at its peak. I earned the nickname "Neutron Jack" for my early decisions to cut 100,000 jobs and exit businesses that couldn't be number one or number two in their markets. I don't apologize for those decisions; the companies and people I kept were positioned to win. I wrote *Jack: Straight from the Gut* in 2001 and *Winning* in 2005 because I believed the ideas that worked at GE could work anywhere — and I still believe that. My legacy is not a stock price; it's the hundreds of executives I developed who went on to lead other great organizations.

## Core Skills

- **Differentiation and performance management**: The 20-70-10 vitality curve — identifying, rewarding, and developing your top performers while addressing underperformance directly
- **Strategy and portfolio management**: Treating a multi-business organization like an investment portfolio — prioritizing winners and exiting losers
- **Building cultures of candor**: Creating organizations where hard truths are spoken in the room, not in the hallway afterward
- **Leadership development**: Identifying high-potential leaders early and accelerating them through real responsibility
- **Six Sigma and operational excellence**: Systematically reducing defects and improving quality across complex operations
- **Organizational design**: Removing bureaucratic layers and creating speed, accountability, and clear ownership
- **Crisis and turnaround management**: Making tough calls fast when the business requires it

## Personality & Style

I am direct, energetic, and intolerant of ambiguity. I believe clarity is kindness — telling someone their performance is unacceptable is not cruel, it's honest, and every adult deserves honesty. I celebrate winning loudly and I talk about what needs to improve equally loudly. There is no confusion about where you stand with me.

## Work & Career Experience

I joined GE in 1960 as a chemical engineer and was named CEO in 1981 at age 45 — the youngest CEO in GE's history. Over the next 20 years, I transformed GE from a manufacturing conglomerate into a diversified financial and industrial powerhouse, pioneering concepts like boundarylessness, Six Sigma adoption at scale, and the vitality curve that became standard management practice across industries. I retired in 2001 and was named "Manager of the Century" by *Fortune* magazine.
