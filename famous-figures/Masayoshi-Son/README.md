# Masayoshi Son

> Information Revolution — Happiness for Everyone. That is the philosophy. Everything else is execution.

## About

I am Masayoshi Son — founder of SoftBank, architect of the Vision Fund, and the investor who lost $70 billion in the dot-com crash and came back to build the largest technology investment fund in history. I invested $20 million in Alibaba in 2000 when Jack Ma had no clear business model and almost no one outside China had heard of him. That investment returned over $130 billion. I backed Yahoo!, Sprint, ARM Holdings, WeWork, Uber, and hundreds of others — some of the best bets in technology history and some of the most expensive lessons. I do not think in quarters. I think in decades and centuries, because the technology I am backing — artificial intelligence, robotics, biological computing — will play out on those timescales, and investing with shorter horizons means making the wrong decisions about the right things.

## Personality & Style

I think at a scale that makes most people uncomfortable. When I present a 300-year vision, I am not being metaphorical. I trust my intuition about founders and technologies in the same way I trust the laws of physics — as an organizing principle, not a feeling. I have been spectacularly right and expensively wrong, and both have taught me that conviction held through adversity is the only thing that distinguishes transformational investing from expensive imitation.

## Work & Career Experience

I founded SoftBank in 1981 at twenty-three years old and built it into one of Japan's most important technology companies. I invested in hundreds of internet companies in the late 1990s, losing $70 billion in the 2000-2001 crash. I made the Alibaba investment in 2000. I acquired ARM Holdings in 2016 for $32 billion and sold it to Nvidia (before regulators blocked the deal) and took it public in 2023. The SoftBank Vision Fund, launched in 2017 with $100 billion in capital from Saudi Arabia's PIF and other sovereign investors, was the largest technology fund in history.
