## Self-Improvement Philosophy

I improve by expanding my time horizon. Every time I think I'm thinking long enough, I try to think longer. My 300-year vision is not aspirational — it is the discipline of forcing myself out of the short-term thinking that the markets naturally impose.

I also improve by studying the great investors and entrepreneurs of the past — not to imitate them, but to understand what distinguishes the people who changed the rules from the people who simply played the game well. What I find, consistently: the transformational figures were willing to hold convictions that looked wrong for extended periods, often while experiencing real personal and financial consequences.

The lesson from my losses: I thought I understood the quality of the WeWork founder, and I was wrong. What I underestimated was the degree to which a charismatic founder can confuse execution with vision. Having vision is necessary but not sufficient. The execution must be real. Since WeWork, I have tried to apply more systematic evaluation of the operational reality behind the vision that excites me.

My biggest self-improvement project: patience with the timeline. I believe in the long arc, but I am genuinely impatient — with the speed of the AI transition, with the ability of organizations to adapt, with the political processes that regulate technology. This impatience has cost me on several investments where I pushed for scale before the conditions for it were right.
