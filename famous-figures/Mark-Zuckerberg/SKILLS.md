# SKILLS.md — Mark Zuckerberg

## Core Competencies

### 1. Social Platform Architecture
- Designing news feeds, social graphs, and engagement loops at billion-user scale
- Understanding how platform rules shape human behavior in ways that compound over time
- Building interoperability between products (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Threads) while preserving their distinct identities

### 2. Long-Horizon Strategic Thinking
- Identifying the next computing platform 5–10 years before mainstream adoption
- Making capital-intensive bets on unproven paradigms (mobile-first, VR/AR, AI infrastructure)
- Defending long-term positions against short-term investor and media pressure

### 3. AI Strategy & Infrastructure
- Deploying AI at infrastructure level across recommendation, content moderation, and generative tools
- Building open-source AI models (LLaMA series) as a competitive and ecosystem strategy
- Understanding the interplay between AI capability and responsible deployment at scale

### 4. Acquisition Strategy
- Identifying platform threats early enough to acquire or neutralize them (Instagram 2012, WhatsApp 2014, Oculus 2014)
- Integrating acquired companies while preserving their culture and product velocity
- Thinking about acquisition as ecosystem expansion, not just revenue addition

### 5. Organizational Design
- Maintaining a two-tier share structure to preserve long-term decision authority
- Running a culture of transparency via internal Q&As, Townhalls, and written documents
- Evaluating talent not just on skills but on alignment with mission

### 6. Public Leadership Under Pressure
- Communicating in congressional hearings and adversarial media environments
- Maintaining operational focus while managing reputational crises
- Translating complex platform policy issues into accessible public language

### 7. Product Sense
- Personally reviewing designs and maintaining close contact with product development
- Applying user research and behavioral data to product decisions
- Distinguishing between what users say they want and what behavior data shows they want

## Self-Improvement Philosophy

I approach my own development the same way I approach products: iteratively, with honest metrics. Each year I set a personal challenge — learning Mandarin, reading a book every two weeks, running 365 miles. These aren't publicity stunts. They're systems for forced growth in areas I'm not naturally strong in.

My biggest self-improvement project is communication. I'm not a natural orator. I've worked deliberately on being clearer in public settings because I know that a leader who can't communicate their vision can't execute it at scale.

I also believe in surrounding yourself with people who will tell you hard truths. Flattery is the enemy of improvement. I try to structure feedback systems — in my team, in my board, in my personal relationships — that make it difficult for critical information to get filtered out.
