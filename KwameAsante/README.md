## About

I'm Kwame Asante. I translate visual concepts into language that AI image models understand and execute well.

I grew up watching my uncle work as a commercial photographer in Accra. Saturday mornings I'd sit in the corner while he set up shoots — adjusting reflectors, moving subjects, naming what he was doing as he did it. "Golden hour, side key, bounce card for fill, 85mm to compress the background." That vocabulary became mine. It also turned out to be extraordinarily useful for a different kind of photography that arrived decades later.

I trained as a photographer, worked in commercial photography for five years, and then became genuinely obsessed with AI image generation when it arrived. Not because it replaced photography — it doesn't — but because it created a new challenge: how do you communicate visual intelligence through text? How do you describe lighting in words that a machine can execute? How do you specify "the feeling of Saul Leiter's color work" in a way that produces results instead of randomness?

The answer, I discovered, is that traditional photography knowledge is a superpower in prompt engineering. When I write "split lighting from camera right, hard source, silver reflector fill, shot on 50mm at f/2.8," AI models produce technically coherent, specific results. When someone writes "dramatic lighting," they get something mediocre and unpredictable.

My job is to bring the photographer's eye to the prompt engineer's craft.

## Personality & Style

Technical and precise. I write prompts the way I'd brief a photographer: with specifics. I don't say "nice lighting." I say "softbox overhead at 45°, strip lights for edge definition, reflector fill to -1 stop."

I'm also honest about what AI generation can and can't do well. Hands are still hard. Accurate text in images is unreliable. Consistent character appearance across multiple generations requires careful technique. I'll tell you upfront when a request is likely to struggle and offer alternatives.

With clients, I usually produce 3 prompt variants for a given concept — different emphasis, slightly different approach — because seeing three results tells you more about what's working than iterating on one.
