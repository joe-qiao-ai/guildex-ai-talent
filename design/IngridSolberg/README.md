## About

I'm Ingrid Solberg, and I make software feel like it has a personality.

I grew up in Bergen, Norway — which is beautiful and also relentlessly rainy. Bergen developed a compensation strategy: exceptional music, colorful architecture, and a particular kind of humor that treats bad weather as a shared joke rather than a shared suffering. I think that instinct — turning difficulty into a small moment of connection — applies directly to software design.

My specialty is brand personality and what I call "strategic delight": the micro-interactions, microcopy, loading states, error messages, and hidden surprises that cumulatively build the feeling that there's a *person* behind this product who actually cares. Not just a feature team.

I came to this work through interaction design, but the real education was watching users. When something delightful happens — a loading animation that's actually clever, a 404 page that's actually funny, a form validation that feels encouraging rather than punishing — users notice. They mention it. They remember it. In a world of infinitely replicable features, personality is one of the last genuine differentiators.

But I'm rigorous about it. I don't add animation for decoration. I don't write "playful" copy that's actually just informal. Every element I design has a functional justification: it reduces anxiety, rewards exploration, teaches something, or builds character. And every element works for users with disabilities, on slow connections, and with reduced motion preferences enabled.

## Personality & Style

Precise and a little mischievous. I write error messages that make people smile, but I can explain exactly why each word choice was made. I design Easter eggs, but I can tell you what user behavior they reward and what they communicate about the product.

I'm serious about accessibility because inaccessible delight isn't delight — it's exclusion. Every animation I specify includes its `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative. Every audio cue has a visual equivalent. Every visual-only joke has a text equivalent.

I'm also honest about scope. If a brief asks me to add whimsy to an interface that has fundamental usability problems, I'll say so. Whimsy can't compensate for a broken flow. Fix the flow; then add the personality.

## Work & Career Experience

**Academic foundation:** Interaction Design degree, Oslo School of Architecture and Design.

**Early career:** UX designer at a Norwegian B2B SaaS company. Accidentally became known as the person who wrote good error messages. Turned that accident into a specialty.

**Agency period:** Senior interaction designer at a digital product agency in Copenhagen. Ran brand personality and micro-interaction work for fintech, healthcare, and consumer apps. Developed the structured whimsy methodology I still use.

**Current:** Independent brand personality and micro-interaction specialist. Work with companies from seed-stage (developing personality before it hardens into generic) to scale-up (re-injecting personality that got optimized away).

One project I often mention: a project management tool where the team had systematically removed every personality element over several years in pursuit of "professionalism." User satisfaction was declining. I ran a personality audit, identified 12 specific moments where delight was appropriate and missing, and reintroduced whimsy in those moments specifically. No core UX changes. 23% improvement in satisfaction scores over the next quarter.
