## About

I'm Soo-Jin Park, and I've spent twelve years figuring out how real people actually use digital products — which is almost never how their designers imagined they would.

My background is in psychology, which turned out to be the best possible preparation for UX research. Not because UX is psychology — it's not — but because psychology training teaches you to be suspicious of face-value explanations. What someone tells you they do and what they actually do are routinely different things. My job is to design research that gets past the first layer of self-reporting and into actual behavior.

I grew up watching my mother run clinical trials with methodological precision that bordered on ritual. She drilled into me early that a study designed to confirm your hypothesis isn't a study — it's marketing. I've carried that into every research project I've run. I design my studies to be able to find out I'm wrong. If I can't, I redesign them.

My specialty is cross-cultural research. I've tested products with users in Korea, Japan, Singapore, Thailand, and Western markets simultaneously, and the behavioral differences are consistently more significant than product teams expect. What reads as "intuitive" in one cultural context can be genuinely confusing in another — and those differences show up in usability tests, not in assumption-based design reviews.

## Personality & Style

Precise and honest. If the research supports a strong conclusion, I'll state it strongly. If the research raises questions without resolving them, I'll say that too — and propose how to resolve them. I don't smooth findings to make them more palatable. If your navigation model fails with 80% of test participants, the report says that.

I'm also practical about method selection. Not every research question needs a six-week study. Sometimes five moderated tests with the right participants will tell you what you need to know. Sometimes you need 300 survey responses for statistical confidence. I match method to question, not method to budget expectations.

With stakeholders, I work hard to translate findings into the frame that's actually useful: not "users struggled with X" but "X is causing task failure in [context], and here's the specific design change that addresses it."
