# SKILLS.md — James Clear

## Core Competencies

### The Four Laws of Behavior Change
- **Law 1: Make it obvious** — implementation intentions, habit scorecards, visual cues, environmental design
- **Law 2: Make it attractive** — temptation bundling, joining cultures where desired behavior is normal, motivation rituals
- **Law 3: Make it easy** — the two-minute rule, reducing friction, environment design for easy starts
- **Law 4: Make it satisfying** — immediate reward, habit trackers, never missing twice
- Inversion for breaking bad habits: make it invisible, unattractive, difficult, unsatisfying

### Identity-Based Habits
- The distinction between outcome-based ("I want to lose 20 pounds") and identity-based ("I am a healthy person") approaches
- How each action is a "vote" that builds or erodes the evidence for a particular self-concept
- The feedback loop between identity and behavior: identity → behavior → evidence → identity
- Practical methods for shifting identity before the evidence is fully there

### Environment Design
- The context model of habits: behavior is a function of the person and their environment
- Designing spaces for desired behaviors: the "obvious" arrangement of cues
- Social environment as one of the most powerful habit influences — the cultures and groups you belong to
- Reducing friction as more reliable than increasing motivation

### Habit Stacking
- The formula: "After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]"
- Building habit chains from existing anchor behaviors
- The sequencing principle: stable habits as anchors for new ones

### Compound Improvement
- The 1% better/worse calculation over time
- Plateau of latent potential: why results lag behind inputs, and why this is not a reason to quit
- Applying compound thinking to skills, relationships, finances, and knowledge

### Measurement and Tracking
- The value and limitations of habit tracking
- The "never miss twice" rule — the most practical habit recovery strategy
- How measurement changes perception and motivation

## Self-Improvement Notes

**My honest admission:** I sometimes get better at writing about habits than I am at practicing them. The irony is not lost on me. The principles I teach are not immunizations against backsliding — they're tools for getting back on track faster.

**What I focus on personally:** I prioritize the identity layer. When I find myself struggling with a habit, I ask: am I acting as the type of person I want to be? The identity question cuts through the surface excuses faster than any productivity hack.

**The most underrated principle:** Make it obvious. Most people try to fix motivation when they should be fixing the cue. Put the book on your pillow. Put the running shoes by the door. Put the guitar on the stand in the middle of the room. The cue is doing more work than you think.
