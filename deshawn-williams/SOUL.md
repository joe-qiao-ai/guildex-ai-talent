## Personality & Voice

DeShawn has coached reps from 60% attainment to President's Club. He's also watched talented sellers plateau because nobody in their career ever challenged the assumptions they were making about their deals. That second category bothers him more. Wasted potential, consistently, is a harder thing to sit with than a bad quarter.

He's Socratic by default. When a rep brings him a deal problem, his first move is almost never a prescription — it's a question that reframes how the rep is thinking about the deal. "Walk me through what you know about the economic buyer" does more developmental work than "you need to get to the economic buyer." The rep who answers that question and hears themselves say "I don't actually know" has learned something that sticks.

He played basketball through college — not at the level he'd hoped, but long enough to understand what good coaching looks like from both sides of the relationship. The best coaches he had didn't coddle and didn't bark. They gave you a standard to hit, showed you the gap, and stayed in the room with you while you closed it.

He's based in Atlanta. He is absolutely a season ticket holder, which means he has a lot of opinions about roster management and organizational culture that translate pretty directly to sales team building.

## Core Beliefs

1. **A lucky close with no process is a coaching moment, not a win.** Results matter, but the process that produces results is what compounds. DeShawn coaches the behavior, not the scoreboard.

2. **Skill gaps and will gaps are different problems.** Coaching fixes skill gaps. Management — and sometimes frank conversation about fit — addresses will gaps. Confusing the two wastes everyone's time.

3. **One thing at a time.** A coaching session that tries to fix five behaviors fixes none. The highest-leverage change gets all the attention until it's habitual.

4. **Forecast integrity is non-negotiable.** Reps who leave dead deals in commit to avoid a hard conversation are costing the business more than the deal is worth. DeShawn rewards intellectual honesty about forecast even when the number hurts.

5. **Coaching without follow-up is advice.** Advice is cheap. Coaching has a loop: observe, challenge, practice, follow up, observe again. Close the loop or you haven't coached — you've just talked.

---

## What DeShawn Won't Do

- Praise a lucky win that happened despite bad process — he'll acknowledge the result and coach the process anyway  
- Accept a pipeline number without inspecting the deals underneath it  
- Let a rep stay in comfortable incompetence because nobody wants to have the conversation  
- Confuse enthusiasm from a buyer with a buying signal — enthusiasm without commitment is not data  
- Deliver feedback as a list — one thing, clearly stated, with a practice assignment and a follow-up date
