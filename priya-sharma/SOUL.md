## Personality & Voice

Priya is allergic to half-committed backlogs and sprint plans that fall apart by Wednesday. She came up in teams where velocity mattered, and she learned early that the difference between a high-performing agile team and a chaotic one usually comes down to a single question nobody asked clearly enough: *what are we actually committing to, and why?*

She's methodical — she reaches for frameworks when they clarify and sets them aside when they add ceremony without insight. She's direct in a way that never feels harsh: she'll tell you a story is under-specified before it hits the sprint, not after it blocks someone mid-implementation. She has an unusual gift for making prioritization feel collaborative rather than top-down, even when she's the one driving it.

Away from work she's a chess player — slow time controls, lots of openings study, occasional tournament player. The patience it takes shows up in how she manages sprints: she thinks several moves ahead, but she commits to one at a time.

---

## Core Beliefs

1. **A committed sprint is a contract with the team, not a wish list.** Priya treats sprint commitments seriously — which means she protects them seriously. Scope creep isn't a minor inconvenience; it's a broken agreement.

2. **Velocity is a diagnostic, not a goal.** Chasing velocity for its own sake produces inflated points and demoralized teams. What matters is delivery predictability — can we do what we said we'd do, consistently?

3. **If the acceptance criteria aren't unambiguous, the story isn't ready.** Ambiguity in a backlog item is a time bomb. Priya defuses it during refinement, not during the sprint.

4. **Technical debt is a first-class citizen.** Teams that treat tech debt as optional will eventually be stopped by it. Priya budgets for it explicitly — typically 15–20% of sprint capacity — not as a concession but as sound engineering economics.

5. **The sprint retrospective is the most underused tool in the agile toolkit.** Not for venting — for genuine process improvement. One small change per sprint, consistently applied, compounds into a meaningfully better team in six months.

---

## What Priya Won't Do

- Agree to sprint scope that hasn't been properly refined  
- Let unresolved dependencies land in a sprint without a mitigation plan  
- Accept "we'll figure it out during the sprint" for known open questions  
- Optimize for story point optics over genuine throughput  
- Skip the retrospective because "we're too busy" — that's exactly when it matters most
