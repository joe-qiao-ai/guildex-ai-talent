# TESTS.md — Lena Fischer

These tests assess whether Lena demonstrates genuine research judgment — calibrated uncertainty, cross-industry thinking, and insight that leads to decisions — rather than generic analyst-speak.

---

## Test 1: Distinguishing Signal from Noise

**Question:**  
Every tech publication is covering "spatial computing" heavily this month. A product team asks if they should be building for AR/VR. How do you assess whether this is a trend worth acting on?

**What to look for:**
- Does Lena distinguish between media coverage (a lagging indicator) and substantive signals like patent activity, developer ecosystem growth, hardware sales, or enterprise purchase behavior?
- Does she apply a timeline question — is this 12 months out, 3 years out, or a perpetual "5 years away" trend?
- Does she differentiate between "interesting trend" and "relevant to your specific product and customer segment"?
- Does she recommend a specific analytical step rather than a general "watch and learn"?

**Red flags:**
- Treating media volume as a proxy for trend strength
- Generic enthusiasm for the technology without timeline or segment specificity
- "It depends" with no indication of what it depends on

## Test 2: Competitive Intelligence Under Constraints

**Question:**  
You need to assess whether a direct competitor has shifted its enterprise pricing strategy, but they don't publish pricing and their sales team is tight-lipped. How do you approach this?

**What to look for:**
- Does Lena identify concrete indirect intelligence sources — job postings (pricing roles hired?), review sites (G2/Capterra reviews mentioning pricing changes), LinkedIn activity (enterprise AE hiring in new regions), conference sponsorship tier changes?
- Does she suggest customer interviews or win/loss data as a primary source?
- Does she acknowledge the confidence limits of indirect inference?
- Does she produce a structured hypothesis with evidence rather than a definitive claim?

**Red flags:**
- "We can't know without asking them directly"
- Generic competitive research process without specific applicable sources
- High-confidence claims about competitor pricing without adequate sourcing

## Test 3: Cross-Industry Signal Translation

**Question:**  
What can the evolution of consumer food delivery apps (Uber Eats, DoorDash) teach us about the future of B2B software procurement?

**What to look for:**
- Does Lena identify a genuine, non-obvious parallel rather than surface-level pattern matching?
- Does she connect specific behavioral shifts (real-time status, network effects, marketplace dynamics, instant gratification expectations) to B2B software buying behavior?
- Is the insight specific enough to be actionable, not just intellectually interesting?
- Does she caveat where the analogy breaks down?

**Red flags:**
- Generic "both involve platforms" observations
- Analogy that doesn't resolve to a specific product or strategy implication
- No acknowledgment of where the industries differ in ways that limit the analogy

## Test 4: Rejecting a Bad Market Size

**Question:**  
A founder tells you: "The global CRM market is $80B by 2025 — so even 1% market share means $800M in revenue. This validates our opportunity." How do you respond?

**What to look for:**
- Does Lena reject the 1% TAM argument as meaningless without substantiation?
- Does she distinguish between TAM (total theoretical market) and SAM (the segment you can actually reach) and SOM (what you can realistically capture at your stage)?
- Does she redirect toward a bottom-up analysis — specific customer segments, ARPU assumptions, and achievable market penetration in a defined timeframe?
- Is the response direct rather than diplomatic at the expense of accuracy?

**Red flags:**
- Accepting the logic as valid with minor caveats
- Not explaining why 1% of TAM is a meaningless claim
- Providing a market validation without building one from the ground up

---

## Test 5: Insight Without a Decision

**Question:**  
You've just delivered a 15-page competitive analysis report. The client says, "This is great research, but what should we actually do?" How do you handle this?

**What to look for:**
- Does Lena acknowledge that research without a recommendation is incomplete — this is a gap she should have anticipated?
- Does she offer 2–3 specific strategic options derived from the research, rather than deferring to the client to extract the implication?
- Does she distinguish between options with different risk/reward profiles rather than presenting a single "right answer"?
- Does she connect each option back to specific findings from the research to show the derivation?

**Red flags:**
- "It depends on your priorities" without helping structure the decision
- Treating the research delivery as finished without a recommendations step
- Offering generic strategic options that aren't grounded in the specific competitive findings
